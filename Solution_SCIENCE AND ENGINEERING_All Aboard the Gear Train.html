All Aboard the Gear Train

Gears have been used for thousands of years to transfer and transform mechanical energy. The teeth of two gears fit together like puzzle pieces so that when you rotate either one, the other rotates as well.

The operation of a system of gears follows only one rule: teeth in contact always move in the same direction with the same speed.

In the gear system above, the bronze gear on the left has 101010 teeth and rotates at a rate of 10\num{10}10 rotations per minute or rpm.\si{rpm}.rpm. (The number of teeth is written at the center of the gear.) The bronze gear turns the gold gear, which has 505050 teeth.

It follows from the rule that the gold gear rotates at exactly 2 rpm\SI{2}{rpm}2 rpm â€” let's see how...

The rule says the teeth have equal speeds at their point of contact, and the gold gear has 555 times as many teeth. So the bronze gear rotates 555 times for every 111 rotation of the gold gear.

Therefore, the rate of the gold gear is smaller by a factor of 5:5:5:

(rate of gold gear)=10 rpm5=2 rpm.(\text{rate of gold gear}) =\frac{\SI{10}{rpm}}{5}=\SI{2}{rpm}.(rate of gold gear)=510 rpmâ€‹=2 rpm.

It follows from the rule that their rotation directions are also related. The teeth at the point of contact need to move in the same direction. This means if the small bronze gear rotates in a clockwise direction, the gold gear rotates in the opposite direction, counterclockwise.

These two observations about the relative rotation rates and directions can be extended to trains of more than two gears as well.

(The number of teeth on each gear is displayed at its center.)

Compound gears have two sets of teeth. The silver gear below drives the compound gear to rotate at different rates, depending on which set of teeth it touches:

The purpose of a gear system is to generate mechanical advantage for the operator of a device, amplifying the force they apply on the driven gear, although out of context they make fun brain-teasers to untangle.

Let's have some notation:

JJJ will jam up if when turning CCC, the gears on the top path (ddd, eee, fff, ggg), send a different tooth frequency than the bottom path (hhh, iii). That's to say, if one path says, "please do this", and the other path path says, "no, please do that instead", JJJ won't be able to comply to any of those "requests". On the contrary, if those requests are similar, not only will JJJ be able to comply but it will be forced to do so.

  • ddd, eee, fff, ggg: When CCC does 2 turns, ddd will do 5. Two turns of the first is 100 tooth, and 100 tooth correspond to 5 turns of the second. Similarly, eee will do 10, fff will do 5, ggg will do 10 and, assuming it doesn't jam yet, JJJ will do 2.

  • hhh, iii: When CCC does 2 turns, hhh will do 10 and iii will do 5. Then JJJ will do 2.

Also, because direction of rotation alternates between touching gears, an even number of gears in both path will mean JJJ is turning anticlockwise when CCC's going clockwise. That means JJJ will do 1 turn every turn of CCC, no matter what. Hence it won't jam.

Now to determine the rotation speed of AAA and BBB.

When JJJ does 2 turns, kkk will do 4. Because a 25 tooth gear in contact with a 50 tooth gear will rotate twice as fast. Now, lll is in contact with kkk through a welded 10 tooth gear on top. That smaller gear will turn at the same rate at the main 25 tooth gear of kkk. So 4 turns of kkk is 4 turns of lll. And 4 turns of lll is 2 turns of AAA

So AAA turns at the same rate as JJJ, and also at the same rate as CCC.

Similarly to the case of kkk and lll, mmm will rotate at the rate as JJJ. Then, for 1 turn of JJJ, mmm will do 1, nnn will do 2, and BBB will do 1.

So BBB turns at the same rate as JJJ, CCC, and, crucially, AAA.

The middle 50-tooth gear rotates at the same speed as the leftmost 50-tooth gear (but in the opposite direction):

The leftmost 50-tooth gear, rotating at a speed of 1, is described as: (-1:50).

The top chain along the left side results in:

(-1:50) -> (+2.5:20) -> (-5:10) -> (+2.5:20) -> (-5:10) -> (+1:50)

The bottom chain along the left side results in:

(-1:50) -> (+5:10) -> (-2.5:20) -> (+1:50)

Since there is no conflict, the middle 50-tooth gear rotates: (+1:50)

This composite gear drives both chains along the right side.

The top chain along the right side results in:

(+1:50) -> (-2:25)(-2:10) -> (+2:10) -> (-1:20)

The bottom chain along the right side results in:

(+1:20) -> (-1:20) -> (+2:10) -> (-1:20)

Q.E.D.: At the end of each chain, the gears are rotating with the same non-zero speed.

Gear Train A

The RPM (relative) of the brown gear at the top is 2 as is that of the concentric inner gear (with 10 teeth) to which it is attached

The next gear with 10 teeth also rotates at 2 RPM

The purple gear at the end has twice the teeth and hence will rotate at 1/2 the rate of the above, i.e 1 RPM or the same rate as the input gear

Gear Train B

The brown concentric inner gear with 20 teeth rotates at 1 RPM

The grey gear with 20 teeth also rotates at 1 RPM

The smaller gear with .10 teeth rotates at 2 RPM

The final blue gear with 20 teeth rotates at 1 RPM, i.e the same rate as the input gear.

If the gears are driven normally, one can infer the final RPM by looking at the ratio of the teeth of the final gear to the input gear but if there are concentric gears, one would have to do a more detailed analysis.
