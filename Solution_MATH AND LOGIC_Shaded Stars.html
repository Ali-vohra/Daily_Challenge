Shaded Stars

When a regular polygon has at least five sides, extending its sides far enough creates a star shape.

What happens when we break up such a stellation into parts? Can we use its symmetry to compare the areas of the parts? Keep reading to see an example, or jump ahead to today's challenge if you feel ready.

Take a look at the stellated regular pentagon below.

How much of the stellation is shaded? Sometimes, drawing an additional line can make it easier to compare areas in a figure. That's what we'll try here.

The shaded region was broken into three parts, but the line we just drew breaks it into four. We can then pair each of these shaded parts with an identical unshaded part (can you prove that the triangles in each pair are identical?):

This means the areas of the shaded and unshaded parts of the star are equal.

Can you apply this reasoning to the challenge below? You may need to draw more than one line.
