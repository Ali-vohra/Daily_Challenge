Take the Scenic Route

Let's say we wanted to trace the shape below, but under these rules:

  1. You can start anywhere on the figure.
  2. You cannot lift your writing tool once you start tracing.
  3. You cannot trace over any segment more than once.

Is it possible? Keep reading to find out, or just jump ahead to today's challenge if you feel up to a similar but trickier problem.

Let's look at a few approaches.

We didn't find a solution here, but our failed attempts didn't prove that a solution is impossible. There are hundreds of possible ways we could try to trace the shape, so blind guessing doesn't seem like the best approach here. When solving a problem, it's sometimes helpful to focus on one piece rather than the whole thing. Let's think about what happens at the vertices of the shape as we trace it.

When we come to a vertex as we're tracing the path, we have to enter on one line and then leave on another, unless we are starting or stopping at that vertex. In other words, each path through a vertex needs two different lines. So, for any vertex with two, four, six, etc. lines, for every way in, there's always a way out. What if a vertex has an odd number of lines passing through it? This means there's a way into the vertex for which there's no way out, or vice versa.

When we start at a vertex, we leave without entering first, which lets us add a single line to that vertex. So, the vertex at which we begin could have an odd number of connections. Similarly, when we finish our path, we draw the line to a vertex and do not need to leave, so the final vertex could also have an odd number of connections.

In this problem, only two of the vertices in the image have an odd number of connections — the bottom corners. So, any path completely tracing the shape must start at one of the bottom corners and end at the other.

When approaching the problem below, think about how you could find the lengths of the curved paths in the figure. What's the total distance around a circle?

The curved paths are then constituted by a total of 3×5=153\times5=153×5=15 arcs of 6π/6=π6\pi/6=\pi6π/6=π length. The triangle can be seen as being constituted by 6 straight segments of length 3. If we sum all that, we'll have a length of:

La=15π+6×3=15π+18L_a=15 \pi+6\times 3 =15\pi +18La​=15π+6×3=15π+18

Each of the 6 intercessions has 5 arcs connecting to it. If the path we want, started and ended in the same spot, we would have needed an even number of arcs connecting everyone, meaning 4 for all. But a path starting on one place and stopping on another, will need 2 intercessions with an odd number of arcs, and the others with an even number, meaning, we can have 2 with 5, and 4 with 4. Since each arc we take, takes out connections to two intercessions, we only need to subtract from the pool of segments, two (not sharing the same intercessions),... and we can choose to take out two of the smallest ones (the straight segments), maximizing what is left. Euler walk in an undirected graph is a walk that uses each edge exactly once (citation: Wikipedia). Which is what we need!

But in order to have Euler walk we also need all vertexes in a graph to be interconnectable (done!) and no more than two vertexes with a odd degree (have odd number of edges going out of a vertex).

But (fig. 1) all vertexes have an odd (5) degree. Which means we will have to remove some edges.

If we remove one edge, we effect two vertexes: their degree becomes 1 edge less. Since we have 6 vertexes, in order to have Euler walk, we can save two 5-degree vertexes (max allowed) and remove two edges to make the rest 4-degree vertexes.

Edges that are present in given graph are of 3 different lengths (fig. 2): 3 units (black), π\piπ (blue), 3π3\pi3π (yellow).

To save maximum length of the path we'll remove the shortest edges (black ones) (fig. 3).

So, length of the max path: 3×3π+6×π+3×4=15π+123 \times 3\pi + 6 \times \pi + 3 \times 4 = 15 \pi + 123×3π+6×π+3×4=15π+12
