Circles and Triangles and Hexagons

Symmetric shapes are pleasing to the eye, so it's no surprise that designs built from circles and regular polygons can look very satisfying. An equilateral triangle is the most simple regular polygon. How can we find its area? Let's look at the equilateral triangle with side length 222 below:

We can split the triangle into two identical right triangles, find the area of one right triangle, and double that area to get the total area. The base of one right triangle has length 1,1,1, so we just need to find its height so we can solve for A=12bh.A=\frac{1}{2} bh.A=21​bh.

It's the Pythagorean theorem to the rescue, since the hypotenuse of the right triangle has length 2.2.2. We have h2+12=22,h^2+1^2=2^2,h2+12=22, so h=4−1=3.h = \sqrt{4-1}=\sqrt{3}.h=4−1​=3​. In fact, for any 30º-60º-90º triangle, the longer leg is 3\sqrt33​ times as long as the shorter leg, and the hypotenuse is always twice as long as the shorter leg.

So, the area of one of the right triangles is 12bh=12⋅1⋅3=32.\frac{1}{2} bh = \frac{1}{2}\cdot 1\cdot\sqrt{3} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}.21​bh=21​⋅1⋅3​=23​​. We can then say that the equilateral triangle has an area of 2⋅32=3.2\cdot\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = \sqrt3.2⋅23​​=3​.

How about a more complex symmetric design? In the figure below, the three curves are parts of circles that have their centers on the vertices of the triangle. How can we find the area of the region between the three arcs?

We could start with 3,\sqrt3,3​, the area of the triangle, and then subtract each area enclosed by an arc and two sides of the triangle. Each of these regions is a sector of the circle with radius 111 that defines the arc; how can we find their areas? What fraction of the circle's area is enclosed in a sector?

Each of the sectors encloses 60∘360∘=16\frac{60^\circ}{360^\circ} = \frac{1}{6}360∘60∘​=61​ of its circle's area, so three of them combine to cover 36=12\frac{3}{6}=\frac{1}{2}63​=21​ of a circle's area. Their total area is then 12⋅(πr2)=12⋅π(1)2=π2,\frac{1}{2}\cdot\left(\pi r^2\right) = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\pi (1)^2 = \frac{\pi}{2},21​⋅(πr2)=21​⋅π(1)2=2π​, so the region inside of the triangle between the sectors has an area of 3−π2.\sqrt{3}-\frac{\pi}{2}.3​−2π​. This means 3−π23≈9.31% \frac{\sqrt{3}-\frac{\pi}{2}}{\sqrt3} \approx 9.31 \% 3​3​−2π​​≈9.31% of the area of the triangle is between the sectors.
