A More Powerful Circuit Design

One of the first rules we learn in physics is that energy can never be created or destroyed, only transformed.

Much of the modern world is driven by transforming electricity into different forms of power. Speakers turn electrical power into sound. Light bulbs turn electrical power into light. Motors and generators turn electrical power into mechanical power and back, respectively. Keeping track of how electrical power is transferred and transformed is essential to making energy-efficient technologies.

Today's challenge involves a circuit with bulbs in parallel, so if you are unfamiliar with this kind of design, keep reading. If you feel ready, you can jump straight to today's challenge.

A voltage source, like a battery or a generator, transports electrons through a wire in an electrical appliance. The rate that electric charge flows through a wire is called electric current I.I.I.

Electrical power is produced as billions of individual electrons lose kinetic energy in collisions as they shuffle along. The rate that the electrons' energy is transformed â€” electrical power PPP in a wire â€” is the product of the voltage VVV across the wire and current I:I:I: P=IV.\boxed{P = IV.}P=IV.â€‹

Light bulbs transform electrical power to light intensity. A bulb's brightness is directly proportional to the electrical power, so the brightness of each bulb in a circuit depends on the current and voltage in the circuit.

Light bulbs (and to a much lesser extent, wires) resist the flow of electrons. When we connect multiple light bulbs in a series to a battery, the current in the circuit is less than if we just connected one bulb to the battery.

Since the total voltage is the same and the current decreases with the number of bulbs, the overall power supplied also decreases with the number of bulbs in the circuit.

Is there any way to add bulbs to a circuit and increase their combined brightness?

In fact, there is. If we put a junction in the wire and connect each bulb to the battery in its own closed loop, the voltage applied to each bulb is the same as the voltage of the battery. We say that bulbs in this configuration are connected in parallel.

The full voltage of the battery on each bulb pushes as much current through each branch of the circuit as if the bulb were connected to the battery by itself, so both bulbs shine with the same brightness as a single bulb connected to the battery.

This means two bulbs in parallel draw double the current from the battery, with the current getting divided in half where the wire splits. No matter how many legs a parallel circuit has, the total flowing from the voltage source is equal to the sum of currents along each leg.

If this were circuit A, then both arrangements would use the same power, and they would output the same light.

Circuit A has 3x the branches, but because the 9V in each branch is split evenly between each bulb, each branch gives out one third of the light of the single bulb on the right. So the total light output in both arrangements is the same.

But the question is what happens when we remove one of the bulbs?

By removing that one bulb, the resistance is lower in that branch, so the current is greater than in the other branches. Power is current x voltage. Since the voltage is the same but the current is greater, that branch will be using more power and giving off more light.

Therefore arrangement A is brighter than arrangement B.
