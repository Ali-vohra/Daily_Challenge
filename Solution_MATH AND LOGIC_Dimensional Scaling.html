Dimensional Scaling

How many dimensions do we live in? Many would say three, some would say four — as Einstein taught us with the concept of spacetime that time is a dimension existing along with the familiar three spatial dimensions. In some theories of physics, there are thought to be 10,11,24,10, 11, 24,10,11,24, or 262626 dimensions! What are all these other dimensions?

But this isn’t a science problem, this is a math problem. For a look at how we define dimension geometrically, keep reading. Or jump straight to today’s challenge.

Let’s unpack the “familiar three spatial dimensions.” The dimension of an object is the number of perpendicular directions that we can use to measure distances on that object. (Confusingly, these directions are also called dimensions.)

In any corner of a room, the lines where the walls meet the floor or each other show three perpendicular directions; these are the three spatial dimensions we live in. Here’s how some examples from geometry fit into those 333 dimensions:

  • A point is 000-dimensional; it can mark a position but doesn’t take up any space.
  • A line is 111-dimensional; it only has length.
  • A flat shape is 222-dimensional; it has length and width.
  • A solid figure is 333-dimensional; it has length, width, and height.

Dimension also relates to how an object can be made up of smaller copies of itself. Consider a line segment. It’s a portion of a line, so it’s 111-dimensional.

If we scale its length down by a factor of 2,2,2, we can put 222 copies of the line segment together to recreate the original line segment:

If we scale its length down by a factor of 3,3,3, we can put 333 copies of the line segment together to recreate the original line segment:

Now, instead, let's look at a right triangle. It’s a flat shape, so it's 222-dimensional.

If we scale both its length and width down by a factor of 2,2,2, we can put 444 copies of the triangle together to recreate the original triangle:

If we scale both its length and width down by a factor of 3,3,3, we can put 999 copies of the triangle together to recreate the original triangle:

What is the relationship between dimension, scale, and the number of copies here? It’s your job to find it in today’s challenge.

Today's Challenge

For example, if the object is a square, it has length and width, so its dimension is D=2.D=2.D=2. Suppose we scale it down by a factor of S=2.S=2.S=2. Then we can put C=4C=4C=4 copies of the scaled-down square together to recreate the original.

Sticking with the example of the squares:

It's obvious from the given data that C=2D+SC=2D+SC=2D+S won't work, since 4≠2⋅2+24 \neq 2\cdot 2+24​=2⋅2+2.

If you play a little with the numbers, you can exclude the three other false option, and once you eliminate the impossible, whatever remains, no matter how improbable, must be the truth (even if in this case I hope you'll won't think the correct answer improbable at the end of this explaination).

Let's say you don't know the dimension of the square for sure. What you do know however, that if S=2S=2S=2 than C=4C=4C=4. And with a little bit of experimentation, you can know that if S=3S=3S=3 than C=9C=9C=9. With the first pair of data, you get D=2D=2D=2 with all four of the remaining possible expression, so if dimension is either one of those, it must be 2. With the second data pair (S=3,C=9S=3,C=9S=3,C=9) however, you get different value with each equation:

  • S×D=CS×D=CS×D=C gives you D=93=3D=\frac{9}{3}=3D=39​=3

  • S+D=CS+D=CS+D=C gives you D=9−3=6D=9-3=6D=9−3=6

  • C=DSC=D^SC=DS gives you D=93≈2.08D=\sqrt[3]{9}\approx 2.08D=39​≈2.08

So you end up with C=SDC=S^DC=SD which indeed gives you D=log⁡39=2D=\log_3{9} =2D=log3​9=2.

This should be enough for a multiple choice question. Of course you can meddle with the numbers with different ways (like as they suggest in the hint: you can take a cube instead of a square with a dimension 3 and with the same scaling factor of 2, and get 8 as the number of copies), but in my opinion, the dimension is by far the most abstract concept of the three and therefore the most difficult to grab mentally, so it's safer to treat that as unknown.

Of course if you are here, you won't satisfy with the correct answer, you crave for the right one. In geometry, the size of a 2D object is measured with its area. The formula of the area of any particular shape is basically always two lenght-like size multiplied together (alongside with some constant value): height times width for squares and rectangles, base times height (times ½) for triangle, radius with itself (times π\piπ) for circles, etc. So in general you can write any area formula as follows: A=l1⋅l2⋅cA=l_1\cdot l_2 \cdot cA=l1​⋅l2​⋅c where A is the area, l1l_1l1​ and l2l_2l2​ are two characteristic length, and ccc is the constant.

With scaling you multiply every lenght in the object by the same factor S, hence the ones you need to calculate the area. So Anew=(S⋅l1)⋅(S⋅l2)⋅c=S⋅S⋅(l1⋅l2⋅c)=S2⋅AoriginalA_{new} = (S\cdot l_1) \cdot(S\cdot l_2)\cdot c = S\cdot S\cdot(l_1 \cdot l_2 \cdot c) = S^2\cdot A_{original} Anew​=(S⋅l1​)⋅(S⋅l2​)⋅c=S⋅S⋅(l1​⋅l2​⋅c)=S2⋅Aoriginal​.

Same goes for 3D objects, only the measure of size is the volume which in general is a product of three lenght-like size, therefore in scaling there will be three copy of S multiplied together, hence the change of volume will be cubic. Actually it works for 1D objects (like lines and curves) as well, but in this case the lenght-like measure is lenght itself, so of course the change of lenght will be linear to the change of itself.

This might seems be a fairly complicated, maybe even unnecessarly involved definition for the dimension of an object, but it come handy when we want to discuss about fractals, where usual whole dimensions are too robust (in fact, the name fractal comes from the fact that the dimensions these object usually fraction). For example the "length" (in the traditional sense) of the Koch curve is infinite so it's more than a 1D object but it clearly does not have area (unless you take three identical copy and connect them into a Koch snowflake) so it's less than a 2D object (its dimension is actually log⁡34≈1.262\log_3 4 \approx 1.262log3​4≈1.262).

At the start of the final hour, our hard-working assistant leaps into actions again, needing a third load of 27 straws, for a grant total of 81.

Testing our formula for S = 3 and D = 4, we get:

FormulaResult
SD S^{D} SD81, Valid

Theoretically testing all the dimensions we can easily visualize, we have proven which formula is valid.

* ANSWER *

For a size (S) of two, the progression through dimensions is 1, 2, 4, 8, and 16, an exponential growth curve. For S of 3, it is 1, 3, 9, 27, and 81, another exponential growth curve. Of the possible exponent formulas for S and D, SD S^{D} SD is the valid choice.
