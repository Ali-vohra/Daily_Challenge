A Surprising Shortcut

If youâ€™ve ever watched a duck swim furiously upriver without appearing to move or a seagull fly directly into a gale without getting anywhere, you already have an idea about relative velocity. Relative to you, the duck or the gull doesnâ€™t seem to be moving very much at all, but relative to the water or the air, they are moving pretty quickly.

But which viewpoint is the right one? Are they moving or stationary? Does it even matter?

Proto-physicist Galileo Galilei taught us it actually doesn't matter. Both viewpoints are equally valid â€” it is meaningless to say that we are stationary and the water is moving, or vice versa. After all, we are all standing on Earth, which is moving at around 31 km\SI{31}{\kilo\meter}31 km per second relative to the Sun, which is moving at around 220 km\SI{220}{\kilo\meter}220 km per second relative to the center of our galaxy, and so on.

But just because all viewpoints are valid, that doesnâ€™t make them all equally useful.

Consider a duck in a river chasing a scrap of bread. From the perspective of someone on the bank, the duck has to make a tricky calculation to intercept the bread, which is moving downstream. But from the point of view of the duck carried by the same current, the bread is sitting still and the duck just needs to swim towards it.

Here the reference frame of the water is more useful than the reference frame of the ground.

If the duck is aiming for a point directly across the river on the bank, then it might be better off thinking in the bankâ€™s reference frame. It needs to paddle diagonally upstream in order to follow a horizontal line in that frame.

The problem states that the boat's speed relative to the water is constant. I interpret this to mean that the speed relative to the water is the same for all 4 routes. This has to be so, or else the question cannot be answered.

Therefore, the fastest route will be the one for which the boat's path relative to the water is shortest. The shortest path will be achieved by maintaining a constant angle relative to the shore. Of course, this angle must be chosen so that the boat reaches the desired point on the opposite bank. When viewed in the shore's reference frame, this path will look like route A, because by maintaining a constant angle, the boat will move upstream when near the shore, and downstream when near the center.

Route A, if the effect of the varying current speeds can be removed, will appear to be a straight line relative to the moving water. Any change to the "angle of attack" during the crossing will result in a path that is not straight relative to the water, and thus cannot be the shortest path.

I think the answer depends on the ratio of the speed of the boat relative to the water to the speed of the current in the center of the river. If this ratio is much greater than one, then we can neglect the current, and in this case the answer is C (the option A in this case would correspond to exxagerated version of the actual fastest route).
