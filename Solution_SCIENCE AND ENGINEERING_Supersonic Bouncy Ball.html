Supersonic Bouncy Ball

Drop a ball onto a flat surface, and it will bounce back up again. Maybe it will return to the height you dropped it from, but even the bounciest of balls will never go higher than that.

However, if you drop a stack of balls, with the lighter ones at the top, the top ball can soar far above its original height. Can you work out how high it goes?

A single bouncing ball cannot exceed the height it was dropped from because it doesn't gain any energy from its collision with the ground. On a perfect bounce with no energy lost or gained, its speed vvv downward before the collision with the ground is equal to its speed vvv upward right after the collision.

But when you drop a stack of balls, the top ball of the stack collides with the ball below it, which in turn collides with the ball directly below it, and so on. In this chain of collisions, one ball can steal energy from the others to go faster. This is how the top ball can go so much higher.

Let's start simple with a golf ball stacked on a bowling ball. We'll work out how much energy the golf ball can gain when the stack is dropped. Instead of dealing in heights, we'll just look at the speeds before and after the collisions.

At the moment the bowling ball collides with the ground, both balls are moving downward at speed v.v.v. Assuming the bowling ball keeps all of its kinetic energy, it will bounce back upwards with the same speed v.v.v. But it immediately collides with the golf ball, which is still moving downwards at speed v.v.v.

Now, the bowling ball is quite a bit more massive than the golf ball. To keep the analysis simple, let's assume that the golf ball is so small that colliding with the bowling ball has no effect on the bowling ball's motion, just as a golf ball bouncing on the hard ground has no effect on the ground.

But there's one crucial difference between the ground and the bowling ball: the ground is stationary, but the bowling ball is headed upward with speed v.v.v. Surely this difference matters. A ball hit by a bat goes a lot farther than a ball that collides with a wall. How do we account for this?

For a brief moment, consider a bowling ball on a collision course with a golf ball, both with speed v.v.v. This is almost the same situation as right after the bowling ball bounces off the ground, except now there's a gap between the bowling ball and the golf ball so we can better see what's going on.

Using the slider above, you can change your perspective of this collision. In particular, you can find a perspective where you are running along with the bowling ball and the bowling ball doesn't appear to move at all. From the point of view, the golf ball is moving towards it at speed 2v.2v.2v.

Now, the golf ball's perfect collision with the bowling ball is just like colliding with the ground. The golf ball keeps all its energy but reverses its velocity, so in the end it bounces back up at speed 2v2v2v relative to the bowling ball.

But we're not interested in the bowling ball's perspective; we want the final speed in our original, "outside" perspective, so we need to flip back again. We see that the bowling ball is still moving upwards at speed v,v,v, so we observe the golf ball's speed as 3v,3v,3v, three times faster than its speed going into the collision.

You can repeat this reasoning for stacks of two, three, or more balls. Just consider each collision in turn, starting with the bottom ball bouncing, and you'll find that the lightest ball can gain an impressive speed from the other balls in the stack.

The idea of shifting perspectives, or "references frames," is a powerful approach in many collision problems, and it also works if you move away from perfectly “elastic” collisions. In more realistic collisions, objects don't always bounce back with the same speed they collided with.

For example, if the bowling ball and the golf ball only retain 70%70\%70% of their relative speed through each collision, you find that the bowling ball is moving up at 0.7v0.7v0.7v and the golf ball hits it with a relative speed of 1.7v.1.7v.1.7v. If after the collision that drops to 1.7×0.7=1.19v,1.7 \times 0.7 = 1.19v,1.7×0.7=1.19v, the final upward speed of the golf ball is a somewhat less exciting 1.89v1.89v1.89v instead of 3v3v3v. This loss of energy in each collision is the main reason you can’t use a sufficiently large stack of balls as a low-cost anti-aircraft weapon.

    """
Solution of the Brilliant problem 2020-05-10 "Supersonic Bouncy Ball"

"""

def number_of_balls(v_0, v_max):
    """
    Calculates the numbers of balls required to achieve v_max
    """
    res_v = 2*v_0+v_0
    num_balls = 2
    while res_v < v_max:
        res_v = 2*res_v+v_0
        num_balls += 1
    return num_balls

V_MAX = 343
FIRTS_BALL_SPEED = 1
