Running Out of Reactants

Chemical reactions are responsible for many of the changes that occur around us every day. These reactions can change the arrangements of atoms, but there isn't unlimited potential for change: new atoms can't be created and existing atoms can't disappear.

So, while industrialists can synthesize aluminum chloride (AlClX3)(\ce{AlCl3})(AlClX3â€‹) from aluminum and chlorine gas, it is a bit misleading to write the reaction as follows:

This balanced equation conserves both Al\ce{Al}Al atoms (2(2(2 on each side))) and Cl\ce{Cl}Cl atoms (6(6(6 on each side).).).

A consequence of not being able to change the number of each type of atom through a reaction is that the total mass must also be conserved: 1 kg\SI{1}{\kilo\gram}1 kg of reactants going through a reaction will lead to 1 kg\SI{1}{\kilo\gram}1 kg of products.

However, an industrialist seeking to produce 100 kg\SI{100}{\kilo\gram}100 kg of aluminum chloride would need to take more care than just buying 50 kg\SI{50}{\kilo\gram}50 kg of aluminum and 50 kg\SI{50}{\kilo\gram}50 kg of chlorine gas. So 40 kg of H2 can react 6,666 times to form ammonia, while 60 kg of N2 can only react 2,142 times to form ammonia. Therefore, ammonia is the limiting reagent.

The reaction consumes 6 hydrogen atoms for every 2 nitrogen atoms. Since hydrogen has an atomic mass of 1 and nitrogen 14, this means that 6kg (6x1) of hydrogen is consumed for 28kg (2x14) of nitrogen.

60kg of nitrogen will consume 12.86kg of hydrogen (60/28 x 6 =12.86). Therefore hydrogen is in excess (12.86<40) and nitrogen is the limiting reagent.
