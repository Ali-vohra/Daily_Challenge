Move the Pips or Gain a Pip?

Mia and Pavel each have what appears to be a standard six-sided die, and are playing a game. They both roll their die, and whoever gets the higher number wins. The game cannot end in a tie, so if they roll the same number they roll again.

We saw in this challenge that if one player can peel off and reposition the pips on their die, they can gain an advantage if the other player leaves their die unchanged.

Keep reading to see one example, or jump ahead to today's challenge for a slightly different question.

If Mia and Pavel are each rolling a standard die, Mia can't win by rolling a 1;1;1; the best she can do is to tie and roll again. Moving the single pip from that face to the 555 face seems to gain her an advantage, since the "old" 555 face can now beat a 555 and tie with a 666 when it would have instead lost. The "old" 111 face can now lose to a 1,1,1, though, when that would have resulted in a tie earlier.

Moving the pips from the 222 face to the 444 face further complicates things. Here's where it can be helpful to organize all of the possible outcomes and their results in a table:

Now we can clearly see that there are four ways Mia and Pavel can tie, leading to 36−4=3236 - 4 = 3236−4=32 outcomes where there is a definite winner. Mia wins 171717 of those outcomes, while Pavel wins 15.15.15. Instead of both players having equal probabilities of winning of 12,\frac12,21​, the probability Mia wins is 1732,\frac{17}{32},3217​, compared to Pavel's winning probability of 1532.\frac{15}{32}.3215​.

Since Mia gained an advantage last time, she agrees to move the pips on her die back to her original positions. What if Pavel could reposition the pips on his die or add a pip to his die?

Today's Challenge

Mia and Pavel each have a fair six-sided die with 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 dots, or pips, on its faces. They roll, and whoever gets the higher number wins. The game cannot end in a tie, so if they roll the same number, they roll again.

Pavel is allowed to do one of the following before they roll:

  • move as many pips as he wants on his die, or
  • add exactly one pip to one face of his die.

Which strategy gives Pavel a better chance of winning when used optimally?

    If you add a pip, the number of ties stay the same, but Pavel's chances of winning increases by one and his chances of losing decreases by one. Therefore, W: 16/36, T: 5/36, L: 14/36. But an exception occurs if you add the pip to the side with 6 pips, which exchanges a tie for another way of winning, leaving you with W: 16/36, T: 4/36, L: 15/36. A visual proof to this is drawing the 6 by 6 diagram and colouring in the boxes in which Pavel wins, loses and ties. By adding a pip to either one of the six faces of his dice (except for exception as mentioned), you are 'pushing' the tie 'block' downwards, effectively exchanging a way for Mia to win for a way for Pavel to win. Thus breaking the 15-15 chance of winning and losing. In the case that a pip is added to the face with 6 pips, you are exchanging a tie for another way to win (as explained earlier). Hence, regardless of which case, your chances of winning is capped at 16/36.

    If you move the pips, you can exchange more ties for ways to win. To do this, simply just rip all of the pips from one face and and distribute them to the other faces. Let us take a simple case as demonstrated below:

    ( 'O' represents chances of losing, '-' represents tie, 'X' represents chances of winning)

    123
    1-XX
    2O-X
    3OO-
    033
    1OXX
    2OXX
    3O--

    When the pip from the '1' column is moved the '2' column, the number of ways to lose does not change because it increases by one in the new '0' column but decreases by one in the middle column. However, the tie is compensated with another way to win. Under normal circumstances, your number tie would remain the same, you would be losing a way of winning on one column and gaining another way of winning on another. When you are using a dice with six faces (unlike the simplified case above which only allows it to happen once), you create more '0' columns in exchange for columns with higher values. As shown in the simplified case above, you can compensate more ties for more ways to win as compared to adding a pip, which only allows that exchange to happen once. A proof by example would be enough to prove this point, I am sure there are plenty lying around here in the answers.

    This does not work if you do not create a '0' column because you are just 'moving' ways of losing and winning to another column, which leaves you at a 15-15 chance of winning and losing. You can only exchange a tie for a win when you create a '0' column.

    Therefore, moving pips on Pavel's die gives Pavel a better chance of winning.

    I highly suspect the "official" answer on the site is wrong, as in the winning probability using the two strategies optimally should be pgain=16/30 and pmove=17/32 respectively. 16/30>17/32, so Pavel should gain a pip.

    I will highlight this part of the question, "The game cannot end in a tie, so if they roll the same number, they roll again." What this means is that the winning probability is not the chance of Pavel winning the throw, since Pavel may tie and win the next throw. Instead, the winning probability should be the chance of Pavel winning the throw divided by the chance of either winning the throw (or 1 minus the chance of tying).

    To illustrate how to find the optimal gaining and moving strategy, I will copy and rephrase my comment from last puzzle: (You can skip this part if you know the optimal strategies for gaining and moving)

    "Each time Pavel removes a pip from a side with 2-6 pips, his winning chance increases by 1/36 and his losing chance decreases by 1/36; each time Pavel adds a pip to a side with 1-5 pips, his winning chance decreases by 1/36 and his losing chance increases by 1/36. Thus, no matter how he rearranges his stickers (without breaking the 1-6 rule), his winning/losing chance remains the same.

    However, when Pavel removes the last pip from a side, his winning chance is unchanged, but hes tying chance decreases by 1/36. Thus, when he move the last pip from a side to another side with 1-5 pips, he 'steals' a 1/36 probability from tying to winning. By symmetry, when he moves one of the pips from a side with 2-6 pips to a side with 6 pips already, he 'steals' a 1/36 probability from tying to losing."

    Therefore, the optimal gaining strategy is simply add a pip on to any side with fewer than 6 pips. The winning chance after gaining is 15/36+1/36=16/36; the losing chance after gaining is 15/36-1/36=14/36. The optimal moving strategy is to have as many empty sides as possible while no side has 7 or more pips. One possible arrangement is to have 0,0,3,6,6,6 pips on six sides respectively. The winning chance after moving is 15/36+2*1/36=17/36; the losing chance after moving remains 15/36 unchanged.

    Recalls that the game cannot end in ties, so the true winning probability after gaining is (16/36)/((16/36)+(14/36))=16/30=1/2+1/30. The true winning probability after moving is (17/36)/((17/36)+(15/36))=17/32=1/2+1/32. Clearly, 1/2+1/30>1/2+1/32. Therefore, Pavel should choose to gain a pip instead of moving.
