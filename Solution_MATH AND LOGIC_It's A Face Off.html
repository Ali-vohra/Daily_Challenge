It's A Face Off

It's natural to focus on the surfaces, or faces, of a 3D3\text{D}3D shape like a cube, but it also has two other kinds of parts. The faces meet at edges, and the edges meet at vertices.

We can't always explode a shape to count its parts, though. If we know that a cube has six square faces, are there other ways to count its other parts? Keep reading to see one way we can do this, or skip ahead to the challenge if you feel ready for a tougher task.

In situations like these, a logical, systematic method can help us avoid undercounting or overcounting. Let's think about how all the parts of the cube are connected. If we can relate the edges and vertices to the six faces, it might give us clues about how best to count them.

Each face is a square. A square has four sides, so six squares have 6Ã—4=246 \times 4 = 246Ã—4=24 sides in all. If we were to make a cube from six squares, we would need to glue the squares' sides together. The glued-together sides form the edges of the square, and every edge is shared by two square faces. This means the cube will have 6Ã—42=12\frac{6 \times 4}{2} = 1226Ã—4â€‹=12 edges.

We can use similar reasoning to count the cube's vertices. Every square has four vertices, but now every vertex is shared by three faces. So, the cube has 6Ã—43=8\frac{6 \times 4}{3} = 836Ã—4â€‹=8 vertices.

We could also have reasoned that each of the cube's twelve edges has two vertices at its ends, and when the cube is glued together, each vertex is shared by three edges. This way of counting vertices also gives us 12Ã—23=8\frac{12 \times 2}{3} = 8312Ã—2â€‹=8 vertices.

By reasoning about how the parts of other 3D3\text{D}3D shapes are connected and using some careful counting techniques, we can determine how many of each part the shape has. Try it on the challenge below.

FOLLOW the SQUARES:

For the cube, each face of the cube is trimmed into a square (by connecting the midpoints of the original face). That's 6 new squares each with 4 edges, so 6x4 = 24 edges. None of the squares shares an edge. Those 24 edges make 24 / 3 = 8 new equilateral triangles after transformation. Total faces: 6 (squares) + 8 (triangles) = 14.

For the tetrahedron, each face is an equilateral triangle where each edge is extended to become the edge of a square. Considered without overlap, that would be 4 (triangles) x 3 (edges) makes 12 squares. However, each of these squares is shared by two of the original equilateral triangle faces (i.e. they overlap) so that's only 12 / 2 = 6 squares. These six squares do not share any edges, so that's 24 edges. Each square shares an edge with an edge of the original 4 equilateral triangle faces. That's 4 (triangles) x 3 (edges) = 12 edges accounted for. The remaining 12 edges of the squares form the edge of a newly created equilateral triangle. That's 12 (edges) / 3 (edges per triangle) = 4 newly created equilateral triangles. Total faces: 4 (original triangles) + 6 (squares) + 4 (new triangles) = 14.

Q.E.D.: The shapes have an equal number of faces after being transformed.

A secret can be found in the summits by observing what are the angles that compose them.

The cube:

By cutting the cube by its summits, all the vertices are affected the same way. They get a new summit positioned at the midpoint of each. By checking any Summit of the new figure, you see that it unites the angles of 2 triangles and 2 squares.

The tetrahedron:

The tetrahedron, viewed by the top, is being transformed, and so are each summit in the same way. Clearly, one of the summits is split into three to form a triangle and the edges are split into two to form squares. Just like the new figure of the cube, each summit unites the angles of 2 triangles and 2 squares.

Finally, because of the same identity of the two new polyhedrons, they are actually similar. Because of that, they have the same number of faces. This is what we call cuboctahedrons.

This figure is named like this because of the cube, composed with 6 squares and the octahedron, composed with 8 triangles. The cuboctahedron is composed with both of them.
