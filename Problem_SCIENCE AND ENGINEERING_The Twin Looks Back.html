The Twin Looks Back

One day, your twin said goodbye and set off on a spaceship at 80% of the speed of light. In the depths of space, they wondered what life is like for you back home and, in particular, what date is showing on your calendar.

Back home on Earth, you were wondering the same thing about your twin. People moving at different speeds don't agree on how much time has passed. This is one of the grandest principles in relativity theory.

But there appears to be something odd here because, from your twin's perspective, it's your clock on Earth that's speeding away at a speed of 0.8c0.8c0.8c. Therefore, according to your twin, your clock is ticking slower, and you should be younger at homecoming.

Physics, which governs clocks and aging bodies, is the same no matter how fast you're moving, as long as your speed is steady. This is one of physics' bedrock principles, and it seems to suggest the paradoxical outcome that both of you think that less time passes for the other. Specifically,

  • according to you, your twin is moving at 0.8c0.8c0.8c and time passes more slowly for them, so you should be older when you come back together;
  • according to your twin, you are moving at 0.8c0.8c0.8c and time passes more slowly for you, so they should be older when you come back together;
  • you can't both be older than the other, so relativity is paradoxical.

We'll see in the next challenge in this series how this paradox can be resolved, but it requires us to think carefully about the effects of acceleration. The first step is to look at how much time has passed for you and your twin from your twin's frame, just before they start to come back home...
