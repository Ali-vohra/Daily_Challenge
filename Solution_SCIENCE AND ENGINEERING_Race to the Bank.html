Race to the Bank

If youve ever watched a duck swim furiously upriver without appearing to move or a seagull fly directly into a gale without getting anywhere, you already have an idea about relative velocity. Relative to you, the duck or the gull doesnt seem to be moving very much at all, but relative to the water or the air, they are moving pretty quickly.

But which viewpoint is the right one? Are they moving or stationary? Does it even matter?

Proto-physicist Galileo Galilei taught us it actually doesn't matter. Both viewpoints are equally valid it is meaningless to say that we are stationary and the water is moving, or vice versa. After all, we are all standing on Earth, which is moving at around 31 km\SI{31}{\kilo\meter}31 km per second relative to the Sun, which is moving at around 220 km\SI{220}{\kilo\meter}220 km per second relative to the center of our galaxy, and so on.

But just because all viewpoints are valid, that doesnt make them all equally useful.

Consider a duck in a river chasing a scrap of bread. From the perspective of someone on the bank, the duck has to make a tricky calculation to intercept the bread, which is moving downstream. But from the point of view of the duck carried by the same current, the bread is sitting still and the duck just needs to swim towards it.

Here the reference frame of the water is more useful than the reference frame of the ground.

If the duck is aiming for a point directly across the river on the bank, then it might be better off thinking in the banks reference frame. It needs to paddle diagonally upstream in order to follow a horizontal line in that frame.

On the widget above, you can toggle between the reference frames of the bank and the water. You can see for yourself that the distance the duck will have to paddle through the water is greater than the length of the actual path it follows in the frame of the bank.

To understand this, just think about how much paddling the duck would do to stay in one place according to someone on the bank. If the speed of the river is vriverv_\text{river}vriver and the duck is stationary for time T,T,T, then relative to the water, the duck has paddled a distance of vriverT,v_\text{river}T,vriverT, despite not having moved relative to the bank. Motion directly across the river adds to this distance.

Things get more complicated if the river isnt all flowing at the same speed. A real river usually flows faster in the center than near the bank. In this situation, a duck may be able to get to its target fastest by getting itself into faster- or slower-moving water and taking a route that looks far from the shortest from above. Through the water, though, a faster route is a shorter one.

Today's Challenge

Three identical boats travel at the same speed through the water. They are all trying to cross to the far side of the river as quickly as possible.

  • Boat A points directly towards the opposite bank and gets swept downstream.
  • Boat B angles upstream enough to follow a horizontal line across the river.
  • Boat C motors upriver for a while on the near side where the current is weakest before pointing straight across the river.

Which boat wins the race?

    No matter which way the the current flows (assuming uniform flow), it is always the fastest to point directly towards the opposite bank. The effect of the current is the same regardless of which way the boat is point at.

    More importantly, we should look at which way the boat is point at. If the velocity vector of the boat is resolved along the horizontal, i.e. two orthogonal vectors one pointing at the opposite bank (horizontal) and one pointing up/down the river (vertical), only the vector pointing at the bank matters in terms of the velocity to the right.

    Greater the magnitude of the horizontal velocity, shorter the time taken.

    It is also important to recognise that the vertical velocity vector does nothing to contribute to the total time taken.

    Therefore, boat A would win.

    Note: If two velocities are perpendicular to each other, they don't affect each other velocities, only the resultant velocity is affected. For example, when we run in a race making an angle of 60 degrees with the horizontal and try to increase the angle to 70 degrees we only try to push ourselves to move more up and not in the horizontally direction

    It is given the boats have the same speed, let the speed be 'x' in their direction of motion. Also, the bank is in the right and thus it is in the horizontal direction. So there has to be some value of speed or velocity in the horizontal direction i.e towards the bank.

    First, let's consider Boat B, it travels in such a direction upstream such that it moves in a straight line horizontally. Now to move horizontally all its vertical velocities must add to zero, which implies the speed 'x' has lost some speed 'a' in the upward direction to account for the stream's motion in the downwards direction which also has a magnitude of 'a' as a-a=0. So its speed in the horizontal direction must be less than 'x'. Let the speed be 'y'.

    Now let's consider Boat C, it first had motion in the upwards direction which contributes nothing in going right and just wastes some time. However, afterwards, its velocity turns in the right direction and as now the direction of its motion and stream are perpendicular to each other they don't affect each other and thus it will travel in the same speed 'x' no matter how weak the current is but it had wasted time while going up rather than going right.

    For Boat A, it always moves in the right direction and thus always travels with a speed 'x' in the horizontal direction as perpendicular directions don't affect each other.

    So we conclude, Boat A takes less time than Boat C and Boat B as Boat C also travels with speed 'x' in the right direction but it wastes time while moving up and Boat B has speed 'y' which is less than speed 'x' and thus Boat B takes more time.

    From a more mathematical viewpoint, the time TTT to cross the river is given by T=0Ldxcos(x), T=\int_0^L \frac{dx}{\cos \theta(x)}, T=0Lcos(x)dx, where LLL is the width of the river, measured along a horizontal straight line across the two banks (the xxx axis) or, more precisely, between the starting point and a corresponding point on the opposite bank with the same yyy value; \theta is the steering angle, i.e., the angle that the boat (the stern to bow direction) makes to the xxx axis, and I have assumed that the speed of the boat is one (actually this value doesn't matter). Moreover, the river velocity is assumed to be only vertical. In order to minimize TTT, it is intuitive that \theta has to be as small as possible throughout all of the trajectory. In particular, if \theta is identically zero for all xxx, one expects TTT to be a minimum. Actually, this is confirmed by the calculus of variation, which I won't show here. To conclude, the optimal trajectory is such that the boat has to steer in order that it always points along the xxx direction, and the minimum time to cross is given by T=LT=LT=L (for a boat speed equal to 1).
