Cruisin' the Solar System

To fly, birds push on the air with their wings. You push on the ground with your feet to walk. But in the vacuum of space, there's nothing to push on.

To leave Earth, a rocket pushes exhaust out of its engine as quickly as it can. By pushing exhaust downward, the rocket pushes itself upward. This thrust is also how a rocket maneuvers in space.

Engineers have worked out the best fuels, the best combustion rates, and the best nozzle shapes to generate thrust efficiently. But we dont need all that to understand thrust, we can start by just throwing rocks.

The more massive the rock is and the faster you throw it, the more force you need to exert. But more force on the rock means more force from the rock on you, causing a greater acceleration in the opposite direction. Thats all thrust is Newtons third law in action.

Lets give that idea some teeth by working out the thrust you generate when you throw a single rock with mass mrockm_\text{rock}mrock.

The change in speed before it leaves your hand depends on the force FFF you apply and how long your throw lasts t.\Delta t.t. If this force is roughly constant during the throw, it follows from Newton's law of motion and acceleration a=vrock/ta=\Delta v_\text{rock}/\Delta ta=vrock/t that mrockvrock=Ft.m_\text{rock}\Delta v_\text{rock}=F\Delta t.mrockvrock=Ft. This result doesn't say anything about you, only the rock. However, the rock pushes back on you with an equal force in the opposite direction. Thus, the force FFF is exactly the thrust you generate as you launch the rock.

More specifically, FtF \Delta tFt is the same for you and the rock. If your mass is MMM and the thrust changes your speed by an amount of vastronaut,\Delta v_\text{astronaut},vastronaut, then Mvastronaut=Ft.M\Delta v_\text{astronaut}=F\Delta t.Mvastronaut=Ft.

Since they're the same, we can replace FtF\Delta tFt with mrockvrockm_\text{rock}\Delta v_\text{rock}mrockvrock and find that Mvastronaut=mrockvrock.M\Delta v_\text{astronaut}=m_\text{rock}\Delta v_\text{rock}.Mvastronaut=mrockvrock. The product of mass and the magnitude of the change in velocity, mv,m\Delta v,mv, is the same for you and the rock.

Using a rocket-powered vehicle in space is described by exactly this relationship applied repeatedly. If the rocket expels mass m\Delta mm each second at a speed of vexhaust,v_\text{exhaust},vexhaust, you can work out how the speed of the vehicle is changing each second.

In other words, you can find its acceleration. However, there's one caveat: the fuel you haven't burned yet is part of the mass of your rocket. So, as you use fuel, the mass of the rocket changes and your acceleration changes, too, even if you're burning the fuel at a constant rate.

Now drifting away, she decides that the best thing to do is to come to a complete stop (with respect to the vehicle). How should she throw the second wrench?

    This explanation initially referred to force and acceleration. That had some problems, so I have adjusted it in favor of a more intuitive approach.

    First note that our goal is not to move our astronaut towards the vehicle. We just want to "undo the effect" of the first throw, i.e. make her "stop".

    When the astronaut threw the first wrench, she experienced a change in velocity. To stop herself, the astronaut must give herself a change in velocity which is equal in magnitude but in the opposite direction. She can achieve this by throwing the second wrench.

    1) Which direction should she throw it in?

    Let's say the astronaut threw the first wrench to her right, meaning her velocity increases to her left. If the astronaut throws another wrench to her right, her velocity will just increase to her left again, and she won't stop. Therefore she must throw the wrench in the opposite direction.

    2) Throw it slower or faster?

    First, let's imagine you are in space and you throw a tennis ball at velocity vvv. The tennis ball's mass is very small compared to yours, so when your velocity changes it will be by a very small fraction of vvv. But if you throw a bowling ball at the very same velocity vvv, the bowling ball's mass is more significant in relation to your mass, and you will experience a greater change in velocity than you did with the tennis ball.

    When the astronaut threw the first "hefty" wrench, she effectively lost some of her mass. This means the mass of the second wrench is more significant in relation to the astronaut's new, smaller mass. If the first wrench was the tennis ball, the second wrench is the bowling ball. If the astronaut were to throw the second wrench with even exactly the same speed as she did with the first wrench, her change in velocity would be more than before. Therefore the astronaut must adjust for her reduced mass by throwing the second wrench slower than the first.

    I think the scheme can be visualised in a simpler way, if we focalize just on the balance of momentum, rather than forces, masses and velocities in separate steps.

    First phase: the complete system has a mass (M + 3m) is isolated and its initial momentum is zero.

    Second phase: the system is split in two parts: one with mass m moving with speed v, and one with mass (M + 2m) moving in the other direction with a different speed (its module is v * m/(M + 2m) , but we don't need this information)

    Third phase: the system is now split in three parts: the same mass m moving as well as before, another mass m in motion (but we still don't know "which" motion), and one with mass (M + m) standing still.

    My idea is about NOT caring at all about the intermediate step and look directly at the final situation we want to reach: two wrenches moving in opposite, and the lady (with the third wrench) to be standing still.

    The only possibility to realize this situation is having the two "flying" wrenches moving with equal and opposite momentum (because the sum must be zero)

    so the final velocity of the second wrench must be identical to v (with different sign) ...

    but, be careful, these velocities of the two wrenches must be identical AS SEEN FROM THE POINT OF VIEW OF A MOTIONLESS EXTERNAL OBSERVER, not from the point of view of the astronaut!

    In the reference frame of the astronaut, while she is drifting, the speed she needs to impress to the wrench in order to let it attain a velocity equal to v, is NOT v, it's slighty smaller, because the wrench already has a non zero initial velocity (that is the drifting velocity itself of the astronaut).

    The speed impressed to the wrench in the instant of the throw, as perceived by the astronaut, is their relative velocity , that is v MINUS the velocity of the astronaut herself, so smaller than v.

    If we want details... the relative velocity of the throw is

    v - v * m/(M + 2m) = v * M+mM+2m\frac{M +m}{M+2m}M+2mM+m

    Im going to disagree with the answers suggesting that we need to create an equal, but opposite acceleration from the first throw. This is NOT a question about force/acceleration, but rather IMPULSE and MOMENTUM. I could generate a very different acceleration during the second throw and still end up coming to rest - the TIME involved in the throw plays a role.

    When you learn about momentum (mass x velocity), you learn that in a closed system it is a conserved quantity. At the beginning of the problem, we assume the astronaut and the wrenches are at rest. (If theyre not, jump into their frame of reference and they will be!) Now, the total momentum of the system is zero. When you throw a wrench, you impart momentum to that wrench (it has mass and you gave it a velocity), so in order for the systems momentum to be conserved (remain at zero), you (the astronaut) must acquire an equal but opposite (were talking vectors here) amount of momentum. Having more mass than the wrench, you will move slower, but in the opposite direction. Now, to get rid of the momentum you just acquired, you basically need to give it all (so you have nothing left and will stop) to something else - the other wrench you are holding. You want this wrench to have the same momentum that you have (which you remember, is equal and opposite to that of the first wrench). This means you must throw it in the direction you are moving and give it the SAME speed that the first wrench has. Now, in the big system, the TOTAL MOMENTUM is still zero because you have two wrenches with equal masses that have precisely opposite velocities. Their momenta add up to zero, and there is nothing left for you. You stopped moving.

    Ok, so if you followed all that, why wasnt a correct choice offered where you throw it at the same speed? This can be confusing, but speed is a RELATIVE quantity. It needs a frame of reference (something to measure against) to be valid. You ultimately want the two wrenches to have the same speed as each other, but the problem basically assumes the throwing speed is measured with respect to the thrower. When you threw the first wrench, you were not initially moving. When you throw the second, you ARE moving initially, in the direction of the throw, so it doesnt have to move as fast as you to acquire the same speed as the first wrench!

    The thing I think most people are confused by is the difference between acceleration and velocity. Acceleration is NOT a change in velocity, but the RATE at which it changes. If the first throw gives the astronauts change from rest to 1 m/s, say, then the second throw needs to change the velocity from 1 m/s to rest. These are equal and opposite Changes in velocity, but not necessarily equal and opposite accelerations. I can change from 0 to 1 m/s in 1 second with an acceleration of 1 m/s/s and the go from 1 m/s back to rest in 2 seconds with an acceleration of -0.5 m/s/s.

    Can you do this with the same arm extension? Yes, but not with a constant force (=constant acceleration). The distance the wrench moves during the throw can be represented by the area under the velocity vs time graph. If the acceleration is not constant (usually isnt for a throw), you can easily have equal areas as one graph moves from (0,0) to (1,1) and the other moves from (0,0) to (2, 1).     We can use the principle of conservation of momentum to solve the problem. Assuming that both the wrenches are of the same mass, the final velocities of both the wrenches from the reference frame of the astronaut will be of same magnitude and in the opposite direction with the astronaut's velocity being 0(Here, I am assuming that the initial velocity while drifting to be negligible).

    Let V be the magnitude of final velocities of the wrenches. Then, the speed with which the second wrench is thrown w.r.t. astronaut is V. For the first wrench, the speed with which it is thrown w.r.t. astronaut will be V+X where X is the speed gained by the astronaut in the opposite direction by throwing the first wrench.

    Therefore, the second wrench is thrown at a lower speed than the first wrench and in the opposite direction.
