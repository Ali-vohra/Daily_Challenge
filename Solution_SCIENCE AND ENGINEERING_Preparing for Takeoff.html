Preparing for Takeoff

Life is a fast-paced game. Cars, buses, trains, and people are on the move from morning through the night. It’s a lot to keep track of.

But from the perspective of physics, tracking a moving object requires only a few pieces of information: its position and velocity at one moment in time, as well as its acceleration. This is the power of kinematics.

In principle, this information allows us to predict the position and speed of an object at any future time. But because the acceleration of an object is rarely constant, this can be a tricky business — the tools of calculus may be required.

However, we have some ways to simplify the problem.

Suppose a motorcycle is accelerating onto a highway. It starts at a low speed and approaches a higher speed.

This is plotted on the graph below. You can see that the motorcycle’s speed changes more rapidly at the beginning of the time we’re tracking it, and it doesn’t change as rapidly once it reaches top speed.

ΔvΔt=5 m/s2\frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} =5\text{ m}/\text{s}^2ΔtΔv​=5 m/s2
Time{\color{#ff5716}\text{Time}}Time

Acceleration describes how the velocity of an object changes with time. In other words, it is the rate of change of velocity: Δv/Δt.\Delta v/\Delta t.Δv/Δt. You can see how the acceleration changes on the curve above using the slider. The plot displays the acceleration over a small, constant interval Δt=0.5 s.\Delta t=\SI{0.5}{\second}.Δt=0.5 s.

But in a lot of situations, acceleration is very close to constant, like when an object is falling through the air at low speed. Or in the case of our motorcycle, it can be approximated as several constant segments, which you can see by moving the slider in the interactive graph above.

In this special (and useful) case of constant acceleration a,a,a, the velocity-versus-time graph is a sloped line segment, and we can use geometry to articulate the kinematic relationships between aaa and the object’s position and velocity at later times.

The first relationship we can discover uses the fact that the velocity-versus-time graph has slope a.a.a.

If the velocity of the object is v0v_0v0​ when we start observing it, then a time ttt later, its velocity vtv_tvt​ is related to its initial speed by

Δv=aΔtvt−v0=at.\begin{aligned} \Delta v&=a\Delta t \\ v_t - v_0 &= at.\end{aligned}Δvvt​−v0​​=aΔt=at.​

Predicting the velocity at a later time is useful but, more often, we’re interested in predicting how far or high something goes over a certain time. The change in position, or displacement, is the total area under the velocity-versus-time graph (((if you want to convince yourself of this, check the case where a=0a=0a=0 and the graph is rectangular).).).

If the object has speed v0v_0v0​ to start, the velocity-versus-time graph has a rectangular part and a triangular part. The area of the rectangular part is v0tv_0 tv0​t and the area of the triangular part is Δvt/2.\Delta v t/2.Δvt/2. Summing these to get the total area and using Δv=at,\Delta v=at,Δv=at, we can see that the distance traveled by the object under constant acceleration is

Δx=v0t+12tΔv=v0t+12at2.\begin{aligned} \Delta x&=v_0 t + \frac12 t \Delta v \\ &= v_0 t + \frac12 a t^2. \end{aligned}Δx​=v0​t+21​tΔv=v0​t+21​at2.​

Finally, because we can often measure distances more precisely than times, it’s useful to relate directly the final speed vtv_tvt​ to how far it’s moved Δx.\Delta x.Δx. It takes a few algebraic steps to accomplish, but eliminating ttt in the previous equation by plugging in t=Δv/at=\Delta v/at=Δv/a does the trick. The result is the third kinematic equation

vt2−v02=2aΔx.v_t^2 - v_0^2 = 2a\Delta x.vt2​−v02​=2aΔx.

Using these three relationships, you can solve (at least approximately) any motion-along-a-line problem you are matched against.

And if you find yourself faced with a two-dimensional, constant-acceleration problem like projectile motion, you can solve it with two copies of these relationships: one for the horizontal motion and the other for the vertical motion.

    During take-off, both planes are undergoing constant acceleration so their velocity/time graphs are straight lines, starting at 0 m/s and reaching (lets call it) v m/s after (let's call it) t seconds. The slope of the graph is the acceleration (which is constant at v/t) and the area under the graph is the distance travelled. By geometry d=0.5 × v × t

    We have the acceleration and take-off speed for both planes. We want to know how much runway each of our planes needs.

    So we have a=v/t and we want d = 0.5 × v × t

    a=v/t so t=v/a

    d = 0.5 × v × t = 0.5 × v × v/a

    For the jumbo, d = 0.5 × 75 × 75 ÷ 2 = 1406.25 m

    For the mid-sized jet, d = 0.5 × 75 × 75 ÷ 3 = 937.5 m

    The runway is 1200 m long so the mid-sized jet will be able to use it but the jumbo won’t.
