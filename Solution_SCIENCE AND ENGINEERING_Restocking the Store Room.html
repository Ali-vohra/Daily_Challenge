Restocking the Store Room

Chemical reactions are responsible for many of the changes that occur around us every day. These reactions can change the arrangements of atoms, but there isn't unlimited potential for change: new atoms can't be created and existing atoms can't disappear.

So, while industrialists can synthesize aluminum chloride (AlClX3)(\ce{AlCl3})(AlClX3​) from aluminum and chlorine gas, it is a bit misleading to write the reaction as follows:

This would make it seem like chlorine atoms are being created out of thin air! There are only 222 chlorine atoms on the left side of the equation, but 333 on the right side. Instead, the balanced form of the reaction would be written like this:

This balanced equation conserves both Al\ce{Al}Al atoms (2(2(2 on each side))) and Cl\ce{Cl}Cl atoms (6(6(6 on each side).).).

A consequence of not being able to change the number of each type of atom through a reaction is that the total mass must also be conserved: 1 kg\SI{1}{\kilo\gram}1 kg of reactants going through a reaction will lead to 1 kg\SI{1}{\kilo\gram}1 kg of products.

However, an industrialist seeking to produce 100 kg\SI{100}{\kilo\gram}100 kg of aluminum chloride would need to take more care than just buying 50 kg\SI{50}{\kilo\gram}50 kg of aluminum and 50 kg\SI{50}{\kilo\gram}50 kg of chlorine gas. To determine how much aluminum chloride would be produced in this case, we need to consider the balanced equation and the individual masses of the different reactants.

The atomic weight of aluminum is 272727, and the atomic weight of chlorine is ∼35.5\sim 35.5∼35.5. This means that for every 2×27=54 kg2\times 27 = \SI{54}{\kilo\gram}2×27=54 kg of aluminum used in aluminum chloride synthesis, 6×35.5=213 kg6\times 35.5 = \SI{213}{\kilo\gram}6×35.5=213 kg of chlorine gas would be used. If we used 50 kg\SI{50}{\kilo\gram}50 kg of each, the chlorine would run out way before the aluminum!

When running a chemical reaction with specific masses of the reactants, the reactant that will run out first is called the limiting reagent. In the example above, chlorine would be the limiting reagent, since it will get totally consumed before the aluminum, limiting the reaction from continuing.

How much aluminum chloride would actually be produced in this case? We know that all 50 kg\SI{50}{\kilo\gram}50 kg of chlorine gas would be used, but we need to calculate how much aluminum that would react with. Since the mass ratio of aluminum to chlorine in the reaction is 54213≈0.25,\frac{54}{213} \approx 0.25,21354​≈0.25, the reacted mass of aluminum would be about 0.250.250.25 of the reacted mass of chlorine: 0.25×50 kg=12.5 kg0.25\times \SI{50}{\kilo\gram} = \SI{12.5}{\kilo\gram}0.25×50 kg=12.5 kg of aluminum.

Mass is conserved through our reaction, so even though the industrialist bought 100 kg\SI{100}{\kilo\gram}100 kg of reactants, he'd only end up with 12.5 kg+50 kg=62.5 kg\SI{12.5}{\kilo\gram}+\SI{50}{\kilo\gram}=\SI{62.5}{\kilo\gram}12.5 kg+50 kg=62.5 kg of aluminum chloride in the end.
