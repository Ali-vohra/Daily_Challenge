How Old Is Your Twin?

Imagine that you have a twin and that one birthday they set off in a spaceship traveling at 80%80\%80% of the speed of light.

Exactly six years to the day, they return home and you're excited to celebrate your birthday together, but they look at you strangely. "It's not time yet."

When things move near the speed of light, our everyday understanding of space and time no longer applies. We can no longer trust that times and distances are the same for different observers and two events that are simultaneous according to me might not be according to you.

Is there anything we can rely on in our relativistic universe that will be the same for all observers? We know the speed of light in a vacuum, or 'universal speed limit', is one such thing. When Einstein was first developing special relativity, he used thought experiments with bouncing beams of light to work out how time and space should change.

But this is a cumbersome process and doesn't seem to help us too much with you and your twin. Does the universe give us anything else?

Luckily, it does. It's called the spacetime interval.

In relativity, space and time are tied together into a new four-dimensional spacetime. Instead of thinking of points in space, we have to shift to thinking about events, which have both a position in space (x,y,z)(x,y,z)(x,y,z) and a time ttt. For a different observer moving at a different speed, these coordinates will change to new values (tâ€²,xâ€²,yâ€²,zâ€²).\left(t^\prime, x^\prime, y^\prime, z^\prime\right).(tâ€²,xâ€²,yâ€²,zâ€²).

Above, I assumed that your twin can change their velocity instantly - that they are capable of infinite acceleration. But we can't really create infinite acceleration - it always takes time. How do we deal with this time?
