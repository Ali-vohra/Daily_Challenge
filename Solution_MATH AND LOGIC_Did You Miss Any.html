Did You Miss Any?

To know how many triangles there are in a figure like the one below, you have to count carefully and strategically.

The most difficult part of this kind of puzzle actually comes after you’re done counting when you try to answer the next logical question: how can you be absolutely certain you didn't miss any triangles? Keep reading to see one way we can organize this problem, or jump ahead to today's challenge if you're feeling confident.

If I claim that there are 27 triangles in the image above,

1) can you find them all, and
2) can you prove that there are no more than 27 triangles?

To help with part 2, think about these two ideas:

  1. The figure is made of three sets of parallel lines, and every place where any pair of lines intersect is either a 60º, 120º, 240º, or 300º angle. 60º–60º–60º is therefore the only type of triangle that we’ll find. (If a triangle had a 120º angle, its other two angles would each have to measure less than 60º.) This means every triangle in the figure will be equilateral.

  2. The intersections are evenly spaced out in a lattice, so every triangle's side length is a multiple of the smallest triangle's side length.

I confess I got the solution counting what triangles I see (Just like @Tapani Lindgren, "12 small triangles, 6 medium triangles, and 2 large triangles"). This is systematic because we progress from small triangles to big in an orderly way, but not systematic enough.

The following way is amenable to be programmable. We need first to have a way of counting all triangles that have a common vertex. Giving the lattice nature of our figure, is enough to count the vertices crossed by pairs of lines started at our vertex, and choose for that pair, the minimum number. On other more complex figures, we might need a different setup (or function of the vertex). The second step is to go through all the vertices, count the triangles associated to each and take out the vertex and the connections where it is an ending from the picture, before counting for the next one. Last step, is to sum all, when we don't have more triangle's vertices. This way, we are sure there is no more triangles to count.

There are 12 of the smallest triangles

The next smallest triangle can be made up of 4 little triangles. By inspection, these each include one unique point of the star shape. Therefore there are 6 of these

The next smallest triangle can be made up of 9 little triangles. By inspection are 2 of these that overlap to form the star shape

There are no larger triangles
