Which Is Shaded More?

In the problem below, we have two different squares, and they are divided up in a way that looks very similar. How can we compare their areas?

Let's consider just the square on the left.

This square has been cut into a 3×33 \times 33×3 grid, and five of the small squares are shaded. While we could assign an arbitrary length to the square's side, then divide it by three, and finally calculate the shaded area directly, there's a far simpler way to approach this.

Consider this: there are nine identical squares, and five of them are shaded. Therefore, exactly 59\frac{5}{9}95​ of the square is shaded.

Can you use this approach to help you compare the two squares below?

Today's Challenge

Below are two squares of the same size. They've each been divided into many smaller squares.

Which of the two large squares has more of its area shaded?

The square on the left has more area shaded. The square on the right has more area shaded. They are equal.

Correct!

    First, consider pairs of columns. They cancel themselves, since they have equal amounts of shaded and blank areas.

    This leaves the last column in both squares. Now consider pairs of individual squares within those last columns. They, too, cancel themselves.

    This leaves you with only the very last lower right square, that contributes to a difference in shaded area.

    Therefore the difference in shaded area is equal to the difference between one square from left minus one square from right.

    Since left side has bigger squares, the difference is positive, therefore the left side has a greater total of shaded area

    As Sinan points out, the “extra” square in odd columns, say the lower right one, is the one that increases the shaded area above 50%.

    The shaded area is half the total, rounded up to the nearest unit, divided by the total.

    Eg. 4 squares:
    4x4 = 16
    1/2 of 16 is 8, no remainder
    Total shaded area is 8/16.

    5 squares:
    5x5 = 25
    1/2 of 25 is 12, remainder 1
    Total area is 13/25

    3 squares:
    3x3 = 9
    1/2 x 9 = 4 remainder 1
    (4+1)/9 = 5/9.

    5/9 > 13/25.
    (or, 1/9 > 1/25, as per Sinan’s).

    Works for evens and odds; Works for one square (1/2 of 1 is zero remainder 1; shaded area is 1/1).

    It's the ham in the sandwich problem extended to two dimensions.

    A ham sandwich has two slices of bread and one slice of ham. A double ham sandwich has three slices of bread and two slices of ham. You can keep making taller and taller sandwiches by adding a slice of ham and a slice of bread. As you do, you tend toward a ratio of 1:1 of bread to ham (2:1, 3:2:, 4:3... n+1:n).

    The sandwich with the highest bread to ham ratio is the single sandwich (two bread, one ham). Similarly, as the number of squares increases, the shaded squares will tend toward occupying 50% of the square with the 3x3 square having the most shaded area.

    Observe that for any n × n grid where n is an odd integer, there are p unshaded squares and p + 1 shaded squares such that 2p + 1 = n²

    So shaded area is:-

    (p + 1)/(2p + 1)

    = (p + 1/2 + 1/2)/(2p + 1)

    A = 1/2 + 1/(4p + 2)

    Obviously, as n increases, p also increases and hence shaded area decreases. So for for n = 3, shaded area is greater. Also, as n approaches ∞, A approaches 1/2.
