Where Does the Ball Land?

If you wanted to find out which pocket the billiard ball falls in, you could try making a drawing, but that approach might not work very well on today's challenge. Keep reading to see another strategy.

We'll assume geometric purity here: the ball has no mass, the ball and hole are considered single points, and the ball travels straight. Which hole will the ball ultimately enter in the animation above (or will it bounce around forever)?

To be precise, we want to consider the situation below: the ball starts in the bottom left corner and is shot at a perfect 45Âº angle.

To solve this, let's think about the table's dimensions and the characteristics of the 45Âº angle. Because the angle measures 45Âº, the ball will always move the same distance vertically as it moves horizontally. So, the first bounce will occur 67 inches from the left on the top rail.

When does it sink in a hole? Only when the ball exactly strikes one of the corners, which means that the horizontal distance needs to be a multiple of 100, and the vertical distance needs to be a multiple of 67 at the same time. Since the vertical and horizontal distances are always equal, the first time this will happen is when the ball has moved 6700 inches both vertically and horizontally. So we know if the ball keeps moving, it will sink in one of the holesâ€¦ eventually. But which one?

When tracked along the top of the table, the ball moves the full length of the table â€” 100 inches â€” so after 6700 inches of horizontal movement, it will have traversed the table horizontally 67 times: 34 times going left to right, and 33 times going right to left. Therefore, it will end its movement, falling into a hole on the right-hand side. We also know that the ball will traverse the table vertically 100 times: 50 times going bottom to top, and 50 times going top to bottom. Therefore, it will end its movement at the bottom of the table. Combining these two constraints, we can conclude that the ball, after a great many bounces, will fall into the bottom right corner pocket.

Can the same reasoning be applied to today's challenge?

The ball starts from a corner of the table.

To land in a corner pocket the ball must travel a whole number number of table lengths horizontally and whole number number of table widths vertically.

The ball travels 1m horizontally for every 1m vertical as the angle is 45 degrees, so at any one time the ball has traveled the same distance horizontally as it has vertically.

So the distance travelled will be a multiple of 6m and 14m, but it can only hit a pocket once

So we need to find the lower common multiple of 6m and 14m which is 42m.

42m horizontally means the ball has traversed the length of the table 3 times meaning it has ended at the right hand end of the table

42m vertically means the ball has traversed with width 7 times, meaning it has ended on the bottom side of the table.

So it falls in bottom right pocket

Each time the ball touches the border, it is reflected (like light in a mirror), if it has no strange spin. That happens because assuming an (almost) elastic shock, the border is only able to communicate a force perpendicular to it and will not change the component of ball's momentum along the border. The perpendicular momentum will not change either in intensity due to conservation of energy. With the reflection, all oriented quantities of the ball perpendicular to the border will change their sign.

Another way to tackle this is to move with the ball, and reflect instead the field where resides its path: instead of reflecting its path where the ball touches the border, will keep the path straight and we'll add a reflected billboard filed at that point. We keep doing that until we get the pocket where the ball enters, meaning, until the ball finds a crossing on border lines:

Some care must be taken to recognize the correct pocket. In the drawing above, green fields marks billboards correctly oriented. In this problem, the crossings are always strike from the second quadrant (from left-up). Being the quadrant, a green one means the crossing is the bottom-right pocket of the billboard. That's the final pocket for our problem (red trajectory). I also designed trajectories that strike the bottom-left pocket (brown), and the upper-right pocket (blue). Interesting, it is impossible to draw trajectories that strike the upper-left pocket (that returns to the original point). Those crossings are always at even coordinates (when they are expressed in terms of lengths and widths of the billboard), meaning, any straight line to it, must pass first by odd integer coordinates... the bottom-right pocket... where it will fall.

This graphical approach works because the angle of the problem is 45Âº (the ball moves as much in the vertical as in the horizontal) and the ratio between billboard width and length is a very simple rational proper fraction (f=3/7). The ball reflects first at 3/7 of the length of the billboard (bottom border), 6/7 (upper), then at 1/3 of the width from the top at the right border. 1/3 is the fraction part of 7/3. Next, it strikes the lower border at 2/3 of the width from the right border. 2/3 is the fraction part of 9/7=3x3/7. This hints the approach to more complicated cases. On way would be to scale the dimensions of the billboard, in a way that will became a 1x1 square. The tangent of our (original) 45Âº angle became t=7/3 instead of 1. The rules are simple:

  • At the nth bouncing in the horizontal borders of the billboard, the ball strikes the upper border when n is even, the lower when is odd.
  • The fractional part of (n/t) is the distance from the left when the integer part of n/t is even, and the distance from the right when the integer part of n/t is odd.
  • At the mth bouncing in the vertical borders of the billboard, the ball strikes the left border when n is even, the right when is odd.
  • The fractional part of (mt) is the distance from the upper when the integer part of mt is even, and the distance from the lower when the integer part of mt is odd.
  • When one of the fractional parts is zero, the ball strikes a pocket corner. The analysis of the n and m numbers will says which.

This looks worse than it is. In our case, mt=7m/3 is integer when m=3. That means the ball reaches a corner at the 3th bouncing in the vertical walls, and being odd, means the right border. n/t=3n/7 is integer when n/7. That means the ball reaches a corner at the 7th bouncing in the horizontal walls, and being odd, means the lower border. So, the pocket reached is the lower-right pocket just like the solution demanded. The total number of bouncing to it will be (m+n-1). Not that except the last, no bouncing in a direction will coincide with the bouncing in the other direction... that would imply a corner, where the ball would end.

The fact that t is a rational only implies that the ball will be able to strike the exact corner position. If not rational, the ball will only strike a corner considering that the pockets has a non null section.
