What Is Round and Spins With Teeth All Over?

Gears have been used for thousands of years to transfer and transform mechanical energy. The teeth of two gears fit together like puzzle pieces so that when you rotate either one, the other rotates as well. The purpose of a gear system is to generate mechanical advantage for the operator of a device, amplifying the force they apply on the driven gear, although out of context they make fun brain-teasers to untangle.

Today's Challenge

Two simple rules:

  • In interlocked gears, one tooth from one gear interlocks with one tooth of the other gear, each time.

At 10 rpm, a 20 teeth gear will interlock 200 teeth per minute (20 teeth x 10 rotations/min) with another gear, this one with another, and so on, until the end of the chain. The rotational speed of the last gear will depend only on the ratios between the first and the final gear. Since the silver gears are equal, speed was going directly to be the same, 10 rpm, no calculations needed (but if needed, divide 200 by the 20 teeth of last gear, to get again the 10 rpm).

  • Interlocked gears rotates in opposite directions.

The first interlocked reverse the rotation of the first, the second reverses the first and so on... all odd gears in the chain will have reversed rotation while the evens ones, will have the same. Since the final gear is the 3rd in the chain after the first, it will have reverse rotation, or counter-clockwise, against the clockwise of the first. Or, if you prefer:

1) Direction

The gears in a system of gears alternate their rotation from one connection to another.

If the first gear's rotation is Clockwise, any other gear connected to the first one will spin Counter-Clockwise. The system in the picture goes (from left to right):

- first (silver) gear is Clockwise
- second (bronze) gear has to be Counter-Clockwise
- third (gold) gear has to be Clockwise
- fourth (silver) gear has to be Counter-Clockwise

2) Rotation speed

There is one constant in in a system of gears that is always the same for all gears: The speed of teeth 'clicking' with neighbouring gears.

If the first silver gear has 20 teeth and 10rpm, we know that the ENTIRE system 'clicks' 20 * 10 teeth in one minute, so it has 200 TeethPerMinute.

From this system-constant value, we can easily calculate the RPM of any of the gears:

- first (silver) is already known as 10 rpm
- second (bronze) gear has 200TeethPerMinute/10TeethPerRotation = 20 rpm
- third (gold) gear has 200TeethPerMinute / 50 TeethPerRotation = 4 rpm
- fourth (silver) gear has 200TeethPerMinute / 20 TeethPerRotation = 10 rpm

We could have also simply said that the second silver gear has the same rpm as the first silver gear, because they are part of the same system, and they have the same amount of teeth.

Every odd gear will move in the opposite direction as the start gear. So the last gear moves counter-clockwise.

To find the speed, surprisingly, it doesn't matter how many teeth the other gears have. All gears' angular speeds are given by 200/(number of teeth). This is because every 1/200 of a minute (or 3/10 of a second), every gear advances by one tooth in whatever direction it is rotating. Where did 200 come from? We get that by multiplying the angular speed of a gear by its tooth count. For the first gear that's 10 x 20 (= 200).
