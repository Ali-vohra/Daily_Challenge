Does This Short Stack Slide Intact?

You're a librarian in a tiny little library overflowing with books. They're on the shelves, they're on the windowsills, and they're stacked in towering columns, which you push around.

Watch out â€” push too hard, and they're bound to fall over!

Pushing stack of books around all day, you're well-acquainted with the effects of friction. You know it takes a certain amount of force to start the stack moving along the floor... but once it's sliding, you can let up a bit and keep it going with less force. But if you let it stop, you have to push hard to get it moving again.

Friction is the force you're pushing against. It holds the stack in place when it's stationary and slows it down when it's moving. Though the books and the floor may seem smooth, they are covered by tiny bumps and imperfections which you can only see with a microscope. These bumps lock together, creating a force that resists sliding.

When a book and the surface it's on are locked together, you're in the regime of static friction. Here, friction provides just enough force to resist any weak force that tries to slide across the surface.

As your pushing increases, the static friction force increases also â€” but only up to a point. When a push on the book exceeds a certain threshold strength, the book slips over the surface it was resting on.

So, what is the 'threshold' at which the static friction breaks? This depends, in turn, on how hard the book and the surface are being pressed together.

When a book is pushed into a surface (by, for example, its own weight), a normal force perpendicular to the surface pushes back, which keeps it from going through. If the book is stationary, this normal force exactly balances any forces that push it against the surface. Like the static friction force, the normal force's value isn't fixed: it's 'just enough' to prevent the object from moving through the surface.

I promised myself to avoid to write solutions for a while, but this problem induced me some thoughts I wanted desperately to share. One thing that has been nagging me is, why the maximum friction force is independent on the contact area? Consider a parallelepiped. The maximum friction force it can suffer on a surface, is the same however side is laid off. Doesn't this sound weird? To move a nail, we need to apply a minimum force that is indifferent to the position of it, either standing on its sharp point, or on its head, or laid down... how came? Do not different pressures matter for the friction?

Actually, they matter. If the same weight standing on different bases, suffer the static friction force, then the friction force per area unit is different for different bases... just like what happens with the pressures on them... they are different for different area bases. And somehow, this works to produce the same maximum friction force dependent only on the weight. Too much convenient?

What occurred to me was a simple model how that can be done. Let's return to the text:

Though the books and the floor may seem smooth, they are covered by tiny bumps and imperfections which you can only see with a microscope. These bumps lock together, creating a force that resists sliding.

So, I picked my magnifying glass and watched the surface of the books in contact (it should be enough, combined with my shortsightedness):

The surface is like a series of teeth interlocked. In this simple model, I'm going to suppose the teeth rigid, and no need to consider interactions except the ones that preserve form. The main detail to fix is that, there is a maximum slop mmm to the surfaces. So, what happens when we apply a force on the top book?
