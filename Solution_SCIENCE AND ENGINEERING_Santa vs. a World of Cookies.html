Santa vs. a World of Cookies

This Christmas almost everyone is going to overeat, but what does that actually mean? Your body has a certain energy requirement for physical exertion, keeping itself warm and building up vital compounds — and it's all fueled by food.

Anything beyond that gets passed or is stored as body fat.

The energy from food doesn't come from its temperature out of the oven, it's stripped out of molecular bonds by biochemical reactions in your body. The overall reaction that handles this is called respiration and it uses oxygen to "burn" organic material down into water, energy, and exhaust in the form of COX2:\ce{CO2}:COX2​:

Food+OX2→Energy+COX2+HX2O.\ce{Food + O2 -> Energy + CO2 + H2O}.Food+OX2​​Energy+COX2​+HX2​O.

Most of the energy that you spend in a day is the heat you lose to your surroundings, followed by exercise and thinking, which bring the total outlay to 2000 kcal2000\text{ kcal}2000 kcal per day. Meanwhile, the average Christmas dinner is nearly 3000 kcal.3000\text{ kcal}.3000 kcal.

However, if you don't use calories, they don't just disappear. While some will be passed as waste, your body is like a doomsday prepper who's always planning for tough times. A significant amount of its unused sugar will be stowed — first by converting the sugar into glycogen in the liver, and then, if unused, into body fat.

Everything in the body comes up to temperature 37 °C,\SI{37}{\celsius},37 °C, including food. If it isn't this temperature when you eat it, it will absorb heat from your body to reach this temperature.

The more mass something has, the more heat is required to raise its temperature. In addition, every substance has an inherent tendency ccc to raise its temperature, called the specific heat: materials with low ccc raise their temperature with just a bit of heat, while materials with high ccc require much more heat to raise their temperature.

The heat required to raise the temperature of an object by ΔT\Delta TΔT is Q=mcΔT.Q=mc\Delta T.Q=mcΔT.

If we ate nothing but icepops, we would have to spend energy melting them in our stomachs. So maybe Santa can divert the excess calories from long-term storage by cooling his stack of cookies before he eats them?

Today's Challenge

It's Christmas Eve and, all through the house, not a creature is stirring, not even a mouse. But there's a cookie.

Actually, there are nearly a billion cookies all told, and Santa's duty bound to consume every last crumb. As cookies have a caloric density of about 5 kcal/g,\SI[per-mode=symbol]{5}{kcal\per\gram},5 kcal/g, this is sure to be the biggest caloric shock any person has ever experienced and Santa is very concerned about the likely weight gain.

But then he has a thought: if he eats the cookies cold, his body will have to spend energy to heat them up to body temperature.

If the specific heat of cookies is 1 kcal/(kg °C),\SI[per-mode=symbol]{1}{kcal\per\kilo\gram\per\celsius},1 kcal/(kg°C), how much of the caloric load can Santa eliminate by cooling the cookies?

Assume that Santa can cool the cookies to absolute zero (0 K≈−273 °C).(\SI{0}{\kelvin} \approx \SI{-273}{\celsius}).(0 K≈−273 °C).

He could eliminate ≈5%\approx 5\%≈5% of the caloric load. He could eliminate ≈15%\approx 15\%≈15% of the caloric load. He could eliminate ≈30%\approx 30\%≈30% of the caloric load. He could eliminate ≈60%\approx 60\%≈60% of the caloric load. He could eliminate ≈90%\approx 90\%≈90% of the caloric load. He could eliminate all of the caloric load, and then some.
