Spring Practice

We trust that railings on the observation balcony of a high tower are sturdy and inflexible, but in general solids are pliable. Even steel can bend, twist, and stretch like elastic under the right conditions.

When it comes down to it, all matter behaves like springs, which is why physicists and engineers use them in modeling all the time.

Springs apply forces when they stretch or compress. Usually, this force is proportional to its change in length. Hooke's law relates a spring's length ℓ\ellℓ to the strength of the force it applies:

Fspring=k∣ℓ−ℓ0∣=k∣Δℓ∣.\begin{aligned} F_\text{spring}&=k \lvert \ell-\ell_0 \rvert\\ &= k \lvert \Delta \ell \rvert. \end{aligned}Fspring​​=k∣ℓ−ℓ0​∣=k∣Δℓ∣.​

The unstretched length of the spring is ℓ0,\ell_0,ℓ0​, so ℓ−ℓ0\ell-\ell_0ℓ−ℓ0​ is how much it's stretched or compressed, which is sometimes written Δℓ.\Delta \ell.Δℓ.

The spring constant kkk can vary for different springs. If you compare two springs that are stretched by the same amount, the spring with the larger kkk will apply more force.

The direction of the spring force always brings the spring back to its original length. Specifically, a stretched spring pulls and a compressed spring pushes.

Δℓ{\color{#f59b0a}\Delta \ell}Δℓ

A single, uniform object, like a steel beam, can be treated as a single spring, but more complex objects need to be modeled as many small pieces connected by springs. In fact, at the smallest scale, the ball-and-spring model of matter treats atomic bonds as springs.

Fortunately, knowing two basic connection patterns can help you unravel the elastic behavior in lots of different springy configurations.

The first is springs in parallel. In this case, multiple springs are connected to the same object, like a platter, and all of the springs have the same Δℓ.\Delta \ell.Δℓ.

The total force applied by the springs is

Fspring=k1Δℓ+k2Δℓ+k3Δℓ+⋯=Δℓ(k1+k2+⋯ ).\begin{aligned} F_\text{spring}&=k_1 \Delta \ell + k_2 \Delta \ell + k_3 \Delta \ell+\cdots \\ &= \Delta \ell \left(k_1 + k_2 + \cdots \right). \end{aligned}Fspring​​=k1​Δℓ+k2​Δℓ+k3​Δℓ+⋯=Δℓ(k1​+k2​+⋯).​

This shows these springs are doing the same job as a single spring that has a higher spring constant kparallel=k1+k2+⋯ .k_\text{parallel}=k_1+k_2+\cdots.kparallel​=k1​+k2​+⋯.

Another common pattern is springs in series. This is when multiple springs are connected sequentially. When a platter sits on a line of springs, each spring can have a different length, but they all apply the same force, Fspring,F_\text{spring},Fspring​, which is equal to the weight of the platter (assuming the springs weigh very little).

The total change in length split across all of the springs is

Δℓtotal=Δℓ1+Δℓ2+⋯=Fspringk1+Fspringk2+⋯=Fspring(1k1+1k2+⋯ ).\begin{aligned} \Delta \ell_\text{total} &= \Delta \ell_1 + \Delta \ell_2 + \cdots \\ &= \frac{F_\text{spring}}{k_1}+\frac{F_\text{spring}}{k_2}+\cdots \\ &= F_\text{spring}\left( \frac{1}{k_1} + \frac{1}{k_2} + \cdots \right).\end{aligned}Δℓtotal​​=Δℓ1​+Δℓ2​+⋯=k1​Fspring​​+k2​Fspring​​+⋯=Fspring​(k1​1​+k2​1​+⋯).​

These springs are doing the same job as a single spring with a spring constant kseries,k_\text{series},kseries​, which is related to Δℓtotal\Delta \ell_\text{total}Δℓtotal​ by

Δℓtotal=Fspringkseries.\Delta \ell_\text{total} = \frac{F_\text{spring}}{k_\text{series}}.Δℓtotal​=kseries​Fspring​​.

Therefore,

1kseries=1k1+1k2+⋯ .\frac{1}{k_\text{series}}=\frac{1}{k_1} + \frac{1}{k_2} + \cdots.kseries​1​=k1​1​+k2​1​+⋯.

These two relationships can break situations with many springs down into manageable pieces.

Today's Challenge

A gymnast is choosing between two springboards to use at practice today.

Springboard A is supported by two springs with spring constant k.k.k. Springboard B is supported by two pairs of springs, each with spring constant kkk (the same as on the other board), but with half the unstretched length.

Before he steps on either board, they have the same height. Which one will be more compressed when he steps on it?

A B Both compress by the same amount when he stands on them.

    Referring to the notes above:

    The effective stiffness of configuration AAA, as the spings are in parallel, is:

    KA=2kK_A = 2kKA​=2k

    And that of configuration BBB is:

    KB=(1k+1k)−1+(1k+1k)−1=kK_B = \left(\frac{1}{k} + \frac{1}{k}\right)^{-1} + \left(\frac{1}{k} + \frac{1}{k}\right)^{-1} = kKB​=(k1​+k1​)−1+(k1​+k1​)−1=k

    In configuration BBB, two springs are in series and a pair of these composite springs are parallel. Since configuration AAA has a larger stiffness, it would compress lesser as compared to configuration BBB, according to Hooke's law. Assuming the weight of the gymnast is WWW:

    ΔLA=WKA=W2k\Delta L_A = \frac{W}{K_A}=\frac{W}{2k}ΔLA​=KA​W​=2kW​ ΔLB=WKB=Wk\Delta L_B = \frac{W}{K_B}=\frac{W}{k}ΔLB​=KB​W​=kW​

    ΔLB>ΔLA\boxed{\Delta L_B>\Delta L_A}ΔLB​>ΔLA​​

    Therefore, the compression in configuration BBB is higher than AAA.

    The takeaway here is that just by adding more spings to a configuration does not make it stiffer. The manner in which they have been added matters since it affects the total load borne by the combination.
