Pour the Water

Two containers: one holds 3 liters and the other holds 5.

The challenge: measure a specific quantity of liquid using only a sequence of actions chosen from the legal moves below:

We can use these moves to measure amounts of water that are different from the exact measurements on the bottles. For example, if we fill the 5-liter container and pour it into the 3-liter container until it is full, we can be certain that we have 2 liters remaining in the larger bottle.

We can also combine multiple steps to find even more measurements. For example, we can measure out 1 liter by doing the following:

  1. Fill the 3-liter bottle.
  2. Empty the 3-liter bottle into the 5-liter bottle.
  3. Refill the 3-liter bottle.
  4. Pour the 3-liter bottle into the 5-liter bottle until the larger bottle is full.

This leaves us with exactly 1 liter left in the smaller bottle.

Today's Challenge

You have one container that holds 15 liters and another that holds 9 liters. The larger container already has 11 liters of water in it.

You can do any of the following:

  • completely fill a container from the tap;
  • completely empty a container onto the ground;
  • pour a container into another until one is completely full or the other is completely empty.

Which amount is not possible to measure out exactly?

2 liters 5 liters 7 liters 14 liters You can exactly measure out all four amounts.

With empty containers as in the earlier problem, we could measure multiples of GCD(15,9) = 3L

Hence, possible measurements were = 3n L where n is a whole number

Since we have 11 with us already, we can measure (3n+11)L, where n is an integer ≥ -3 (since for n < -4, amount becomes negative which is not possible)

3n+11 = 2,5,7,14

n = -3,-2,-4/3,1

Since -4/3 is not an integer, we can't measure 7L.

Note: if you initially throw away this 11L, you wil have the same situation as that in the earlier problem. Hence, all conversions should be such that when you fill a container from tap, it should be completely empty and when you dump a container on ground, it should be completely full. In this way amount of water with us would always be = 11 + 15a + 9b = 11 + 3(5a+3b) = 11 + 3n where a,b,n are integers

On a previous similar problem, I showed how we could represent the quantities of water in containers through points in a rectangle of 9x15. I also showed how the several allowed operations move the points to the borders of the rectangle:

What we do to solve this problem is to start from one point, and apply the operations of point translation continuously until the quantities starts to repeat themselves. All the points obtained will be then the only quantities reachable starting from the original quantity:

In the last problem, were the containers were empty, we already showed it is possible to get any quantity multiple of 3 (first rectangle). We now add to it, a rectangle with points reachable only through having 11L already in the big container. These, as we can see, are quantities of the form (3N-1). Of course, quantities of the form (3N) are also reachable emptying or filling completely all containers... that's the result of the previous problem.

In conclusion, only quantities of the form (3N+1) are not reachable... quantities like 7 liters.

Since the greatest common factor of 9 and 15 is 3, from the given rules you can either add or subtract your amount by a multiple of 3, dump everything out and reset your amount to 0, or completely fill the container and reset your amount to either 9 or 15.

Since 0, 9, and 15 are multiples of 3, and 11 is 2 more than a multiple of 3, and since we can only add or subtract by a multiple of 3, the final amount must also either be a multiple of 3 or 2 more than a multiple of 3.

However, 7 is 1 more than a multiple of 3, so it is not possible to measure 7 out exactly.
