Tipsy Tomes

You're a librarian in a tiny little library overflowing with books. They're on the shelves, they're on the windowsills, and they're stacked in towering columns, which you push around.

Watch out â€” push too hard, and they're bound to fall over!

Pushing stack of books around all day, you're well-acquainted with the effects of friction. You know it takes a certain amount of force to start the stack moving along the floor... but once it's sliding, you can let up a bit and keep it going with less force. But if you let it stop, you have to push hard to get it moving again.

Friction is the force you're pushing against. It holds the stack in place when it's stationary and slows it down when it's moving. Though the books and the floor may seem smooth, they are covered by tiny bumps and imperfections which you can only see with a microscope. These bumps lock together, creating a force that resists sliding.

When a book and the surface it's on are locked together, you're in the regime of static friction. Here, friction provides just enough force to resist any weak force that tries to slide across the surface.

As your pushing increases, the static friction force increases also â€” but only up to a point. When a push on the book exceeds a certain threshold strength, the book slips over the surface it was resting on.

So, what is the 'threshold' at which the static friction breaks? This depends, in turn, on how hard the book and the surface are being pressed together.

When a book is pushed into a surface (by, for example, its own weight), a normal force perpendicular to the surface pushes back, which keeps it from going through. If the book is stationary, this normal force exactly balances any forces that push it against the surface. Like the static friction force, the normal force's value isn't fixed: it's 'just enough' to prevent the object from moving through the surface.

I believe you are applying the maximum coefficient of static friction incorrectly in the diagram where you conclude the total acceleration of the second book is 0.1g to the left, given an acceleration of the book 1 to the right at 1 m/s^2. In the limiting case, where the coefficient of friction between the books 1 & 2 is zero, the second book simply has no acceleration, and all the internal friction forces between pairs of books above it are zero as well. This means the bottom book just slides out from under the pile, and the pile remains perfectly still. The second book is not going to launch toward the person pushing on the first book.

In any real case some friction will be present between book 1 and book 2 and book 2 will be drug along with book 1 at some fraction of the acceleration. Since the coefficient of static friction increases from zero to a maximum ( its basically a linear function of acceleration), the internal forces between all the books above it are some fraction of what you have labeled. That's why you get crazy things like pushing a book to the right causes the second book to accelerate to the left.

To start, treat the top three books as a unit, a single superbook with mass 3xM or 3M. The normal force at the interface between the superbook and the bottom book is 3Mg = 3xMx9.8 = 29.4M. The maximum static friction at that interface is 2.94M since the coefficient of static friction is 0.1.

The force needed to accelerate the superbook at an acceleration of 1 m/s^2 is calculated by F=ma. Therefore, the force is 3M.

Since the force required to accelerate the top stack of three books (the superbook) at 1 m/s^2 is greater than the maximum static friction force at the interface between the superbook and the bottom book, the stack slides at that interface.

A similar calculation should be performed at the other two interfaces in the stack of books.

Consider the importance of where you push on the stack to slide it. For a tall stack, you would also have to think about the toque balance because you could tip the stack.

Consider what might happen when you stop pushing on the stack and it comes to a stop due to friction with the floor. If you have ever tried this in real life, you know that different books in the stack might slide different amounts.
