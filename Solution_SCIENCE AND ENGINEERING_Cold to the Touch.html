Cold to the Touch

Heat is constantly flowing all around us: a roaring fire transfers heat to its surroundings, a mother cow transfers heat to her cuddling calves, and heat transfers between your skin and the air that it touches.

One way that heat flows is by conduction: heat flowing through matter by way of direct contact. When you hold a mug of hot tea or a glass of ice water, heat conducts through the wall of the cup between your hand and the liquid.

The net conductive heat flow QQQ through an object is proportional to the difference in temperatures across the object and the inherent thermal conductivity kkk of the object, a measure of how readily the material conducts heat:

Q∝k×ΔT.Q \propto k\times \Delta T.Q∝k×ΔT.

One place you see this relationship in action is when you wrap yourself in a blanket on a chilly day. Unless you're exercising, your body releases a more or less constant flow of heat to its surroundings.

But when you wrap yourself in a blanket, you're introducing a barrier to heat flow, corresponding to a lower k.k.k. In turn, less heat escapes, raising your skin temperature until the larger ΔT\Delta TΔT across the blanket balances out the lower k.k.k.

Since your skin temperature has risen, thermoreceptors in your skin sense the increase in temperature and you feel nice and toasty.

Today's Challenge

You're soaking in a hot tub when you spill some ice from your drink. The ice that lands on the nearby wooden deck melts slower than the ice that lands on the tile patio, even though both surfaces have the same temperature of 20 °C.\SI{20}{\celsius}.20 °C. When you step out of the tub, which surface will feel colder to your feet? (You avoid stepping on the parts where the ice landed.)

The tile patio will feel colder. The wood deck will feel colder. They're the same temperature, so they'll feel the same.

Correct!

    The first step to solving this problem is to understand why the ice melts at different rates on the two surfaces. For the ice to melt, heat must be transferred to it, and if the ice on the tile patio melts faster, that means the tile transfers heat to the ice more quickly. Since the tile and wood are at the same temperature, the tile must have a higher thermal conductivity if it's transferring heat faster.

    Both the tile and wood transfer heat to the ice since they are at 20 °C\SI{20}{\celsius}20 °C, which is higher than the temperature of ice. But if you've been soaking in a hot tub, your feet will be at a higher temperature than 20 °C\SI{20}{\celsius}20 °C, and will transfer heat to the surfaces you're stepping on. Since tile has a higher thermal conductivity, your foot will transfer heat to the tile more quickly than to the wood. Since your foot that's on the tile patio is transferring heat away more quickly, the tile patio will feel colder, even though both surfaces are the same temperature.

    I do not think that our bodies sense temperature. I think we sense heat flow: its direction and rate. So something that is colder than us will feel cold because heat flows from us into it. The colder it is the colder it will feel (because heat will flow faster). But also, the better a thermal conductor it is, the colder it will feel (because that, too will cause heat to flow faster).

    That’s why the metal door handle feels colder than the wooden door between two rooms, even if the rooms, the door and the handle are all at the same temperature. Rooms that are not saunas are below body temperature so anything we touch will feel cool or cold. In this case, the temperature we perceive a surface to be will be a measure of its thermal conductivity not its temperature. Note: this is not true of exterior doors in winter. In that case, because the door handle is a better conductor than the wooden door it is actually colder.

    Also think about the kids experiment with three bowls of water: hot, warm, cold. You put one hand in hot water for a minute, one in cold for a minute then put both in the same bowl of warm water. We perceive the bowl of warm water as hot with one hand, cold with the other. This has been explained to me that each has ‘got used to’ the temperature it was in. I prefer to think that body temperature has locally temporarily been changed and the heat flow from the warm water will be in opposite directions in each hand. That is until both hands return to normal body temperature.

    It should be noted that this is reasoning based on personal observation and that I don’t know the biological systems that underpin this sense. That knowledge might refine or change my belief.
