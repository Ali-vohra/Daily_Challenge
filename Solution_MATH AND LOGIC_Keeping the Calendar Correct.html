Keeping the Calendar Correct

How long is a year? The answer â€œ365â€œ365â€œ365 days""" might immediately come to mind, but a year is actually a bit longer than that. Because the earth's rotation doesn't quite sync up with its orbit around the sun, a solar year is not an integer number of days, but rather about 365.2422365.2422365.2422 days. After even more time, we would see autumn happening in months we normally think of as spring, and vice versa. We call this calendar drift since the measured calendar year drifts away from the actual solar year over time. Calendar drift is very bad if we're relying on our calendar to help to determine when to plant or harvest crops, among other things.

People from many different cultures have invented ways to account for calendar drift throughout history, but we're going to focus on a few here. Around 200200200 B.C., the Egyptian calendar had 365365365 days every year, but it was known that a solar year was approximately 365.25365.25365.25 days long. An attempt was made to add a day to the calendar year once every four years to correct for calendar drift, but it didn't catch on.

Meanwhile, the Roman calendar year had 355355355 days. To correct for calendar drift, an additional month was supposed to be inserted between February and March every two to three years. The person in charge of the calendar would sometimes decide to insert the month just to keep his friends in office longer, or decide not to insert the additional month to get his enemies out of office sooner. This made things very unpredictable, and people far away from Rome often didn't even know the current date.

When Julius Caesar came to power, he decided the calendar needed to be less confusing and more predictable. In 454545 B.C. he adopted the method that hadn't caught on in Egypt, making each calendar year 365365365 days, but adding one day every four years. This had the effect of adding 14\frac1441â€‹ of a day per year over time.

While every calendar year still had an integer number of days, the average number of days in a calendar was now

This was much better than the previous calendar, but people found later that it still drifted from the solar year, since the solar year is actually closer to 365.2422365.2422365.2422 days. The average Julian day was too long, and by 158215821582 the Julian calendar had drifted about 101010 days from the solar year. Pope Gregory XIII decreed that 101010 days would be skipped to put the calendar back on track, and introduced what we now call the Gregorian calendar. It follows these rules:

  • Start with 365 days in a year.
  • If the year is a multiple of 4,4,4, add a day unless it's also a multiple of 100.100.100.
  • If the year is a multiple of 400,400,400, then add a day even though it's a multiple of 100.100.100.
