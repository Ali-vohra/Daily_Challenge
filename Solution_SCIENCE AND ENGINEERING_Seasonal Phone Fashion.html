Seasonal Phone Fashion

Heat is constantly flowing all around us: a roaring fire transfers heat to its surroundings, a mother cow transfers heat to her cuddling calves, and heat transfers between your skin and the air that it touches.

One way that heat flows is by conduction: heat flowing through matter by way of direct contact. When you hold a mug of hot tea or a glass of ice water, heat conducts through the wall of the cup between your hand and the liquid.

The net conductive heat flow QQQ through an object is proportional to the difference in temperatures across the object and the inherent thermal conductivity kkk of the object, a measure of how readily the material conducts heat:

Q‚àùk√óŒîT.Q \propto k\times \Delta T.Q‚àùk√óŒîT.

One place you see this relationship in action is when you wrap yourself in a blanket on a chilly day. Unless you're exercising, your body releases a more or less constant flow of heat to its surroundings.

But when you wrap yourself in a blanket, you're introducing a barrier to heat flow, corresponding to a lower k.k.k. In turn, less heat escapes, raising your skin temperature until the larger ŒîT\Delta TŒîT across the blanket balances out the lower k.k.k.

Since your skin temperature has risen, thermoreceptors in your skin sense the increase in temperature and you feel nice and toasty.

Today's Challenge

A smartphone generates heat while it is on, which means that its equilibrium temperature will always be higher than the temperature of its surroundings. Without an insulating case, the phone's equilibrium temperature will be very close to the temperature of its surroundings, since there's little thermal resistance between the phone and its surroundings.

However, if we put the phone in a thermally insulating case, the equilibrium temperature of the phone becomes much higher than the temperature of the surroundings: Q‚àùk√óŒîT,Q \propto k \times \Delta T,Q‚àùk√óŒîT, and we've kept QQQ the same (the phone still generates the same amount of heat) while reducing kkk (the case provides a barrier to heat flow), which means that ŒîT\Delta TŒîT must increase.

The equilibrium temperature increasing relative to the temperature of surroundings would be helpful in cold weather but not in hot weather. This is analogous to you bundling up in a thick jacket: it will help keep you warm in cold weather, but it won't help keep you cool in hot weather.

Some points of clarification:

Does an insulating case guarantee that a phone is immune to cold weather? No, it's still possible that it's so cold out that even with the case, the phone's temperature will drop to the point where it needs to turn off. But an insulating case at least has a chance of working.

Can an insulating case ever help in hot weather? In the short term, while the surroundings are hotter than the phone, an insulating case would help. However, since the phone is generating heat itself, even if it is insulated from hotter surroundings it will be heating itself up, so eventually it would have to turn off (or be removed from the hot environment).
