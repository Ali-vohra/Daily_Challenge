Collision of the Clones

Drop a ball onto a flat surface, and it will bounce back up again. Maybe it will return to the height you dropped it from, but even the bounciest of balls will never go higher than that.

However, if you drop a stack of balls, with the lighter ones at the top, the top ball can soar far above its original height. Can you work out how high it goes?

A single bouncing ball cannot exceed the height it was dropped from because it doesn't gain any energy from its collision with the ground. On a perfect bounce with no energy lost or gained, its speed vvv downward before the collision with the ground is equal to its speed vvv upward right after the collision.

But when you drop a stack of balls, the top ball of the stack collides with the ball below it, which in turn collides with the ball directly below it, and so on. In this chain of collisions, one ball can steal energy from the others to go faster. This is how the top ball can go so much higher.

Let's start simple with a golf ball stacked on a bowling ball. We'll work out how much energy the golf ball can gain when the stack is dropped. Instead of dealing in heights, we'll just look at the speeds before and after the collisions.

At the moment the bowling ball collides with the ground, both balls are moving downwards at speed v.v.v. Assuming the bowling ball keeps all of its kinetic energy, it will bounce back upwards with the same speed v.v.v. But it immediately collides with the golf ball, which is still moving downwards at speed v.v.v.

Now, the bowling ball is quite a bit more massive than the golf ball. To keep the analysis simple, let's assume that the golf ball is so small that colliding with the bowling ball has no effect on the bowling ball's motion, just as a golf ball bouncing on the hard ground has no effect on the ground.

But there's one crucial difference between the ground and the bowling ball: the ground is stationary, but the bowling ball is headed upward with speed v.v.v. Surely this difference matters. A ball hit by a bat goes a lot farther than a ball that collides with a wall. How do we account for this?

For a brief moment, consider a bowling ball on a collision course with a golf ball, both with speed v.v.v. This is almost the same situation as right after the bowling ball bounces off the ground, except now there's a gap between the bowling ball and the golf ball so we can better see what's going on.

We can think of this problem in terms of the conservation of momentum.

When two objects of equivalent mass and speed collide, with velocity vectors in opposing directions, they will be forced to stop. This can be applied to this situation with the many balls.

When the first ball bounces back, it hits the second ball and their velocities 'cancel'.

The third ball will then hit this ball and gain some upwards velocity, but this can never be more than 1m/s upwards due to the conservation of momentum of this third ball. The fourth ball will then hit the third ball and their velocity vectors will cancel. This continues on for however many balls you add.
