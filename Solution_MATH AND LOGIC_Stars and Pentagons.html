Stars and Pentagons

We saw in this challenge that when we extend the sides of a regular pentagon long enough, we get a star shape.

We can connect the points of the star to make a larger regular pentagon, and then extend its sides to make another star. This pattern keeps going as long as we want it to.

How are the side lengths of the pentagons and stars related? Would we see any patterns if we kept drawing these shapes larger and larger? Keep reading to see a hint, or jump ahead to today's challenge if you think you already know.

Before trying to understand the pattern as a whole, it might be helpful to start closer to the beginning. Let's go back to where we drew the second pentagon and label some lengths. We'll say a side of the small pentagon is 111 unit. We don't know the star's side lengths, so we'll use the symbol x.x.x.

Breaking up a complex figure into smaller shapes, like triangles, can help us better understand what's going on. The small triangle's short side forms a side of the small pentagon, which has length 1,1,1, while its long side forms a side of the star of length x.x.x. Meanwhile, the large triangle's short side also has length xxx, while its long side is formed from a pair of sides from the pentagon and the star. This means the long side has length 1+x.1 + x.1+x.

Let's look at these triangles another way: they are similar, so they have the same shape. (Can you prove this?)
