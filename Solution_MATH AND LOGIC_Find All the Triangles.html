1) Let's count triangles that only contain one pentagon's vertex. Let's limit to the top vertex only. Only one triangle can be created using top vertex. We have 5 vertices, so that's 5 triangles.

2) Let's count triangles that contain 2 vertices of the pentagon, limit to contain top vertex and one other vertex. There are 8 triangles for each vertex, multiplied by 5 vertices equals to 40 triangles. But since each triangle is repeated for two vertices, we divide 40 by 2, so that's 20 more triangles, and 25 up until here.

3) Now let's count triangles that contain 3 vertices of the pentagon, again, limit to having top vertex as one of its vertices. There are 6 triangles, that contain top vertex and also 2 other vertices of the pentagon, multiplied by 5 vertices of pentagon equals to 30 triangles, but since each is repeated for 3 vertices, divided by 3 equals to 10 unique triangles that contain 3 vertices of the pentagon.

So,
- Zero triangles that have no vertex of pentagon in their vertices.
- 5 triangles that have only one vertex of pentagon in their vertices.
- 20 triangles that have two and only two vertices of the pentagon in their vertices.
- 10 triangles that have three (all of their vertices) in the pentagon's vertices.
That's 35.

The triangles in two sets cover one region; two sets contain triangles that cover two regions; and there are two sets of triangles that cover three regions. Finally there is one set of triangles that cover five regions. So 35 triangles in all.
