Math and Logic

Pour It Out

Two containers: one holds 3 liters and the other holds 5.

The challenge: measure a specific quantity of liquid using only a sequence of actions chosen from the legal moves below.

We can use these moves to measure amounts of water that are different from the exact measurements on the bottles. For example, if we fill the 5-liter container and pour it into the 3-liter container until it is full, we can be certain that we have 2 liters remaining in the larger bottle.

We can also combine multiple steps to find even more measurements. For example, we can measure out 1 liter by doing the following:

  1. Fill the 3-liter bottle.
  2. Empty the 3-liter bottle into the 5-liter bottle.
  3. Refill the 3-liter bottle.
  4. Pour the 3-liter bottle into the 5-liter bottle until the larger bottle is full.

This leaves us with exactly 1 liter left in the smaller bottle.

Today's Challenge

You have one container that holds 15 liters and another that holds 9 liters.

Using only these moves,

  • completely fill a container from the tap;
  • completely empty a container onto the ground;
  • pour a container into another until one is completely full or the other is completely empty,

which amount is not possible to measure out exactly?

1 liter 3 liters 6 liters 12 liters It is possible to exactly measure out all four amounts.

  2. Pour the water from the 9L container into the 15L container.

  3. Fill up the 9L container.

  1. Fill up the 15L container.

  2. Pour the water from the 15L container into the 9L container.

The 15L container has 6L of water.

12 Liters:

Repeat the steps to get 3L of water in the 9L container.

5. Empty the 15L container.

6. Pour the water from the 9L container into the 15L container.

7. Fill up the 9L container.

8. Pour the water from the 9L container into the 15L container.

The 15L container has 12L of water.

There is a pattern that the amount of water inside of a container is always a multiple of 3.

Because 1 is not a multiple of 3, we know that 1 is the only answer that is not possible.

1 Litre

Since both containers contain a multiple of three litres, all additions and differences will be a multiple of three. Therefore the numbers 1,2,4,5,7,8,10,11... etc will be impossible.

The number theory course covers this by showing the you can only measure out multiples of the greatest common divisor of the two measurements. gcd(15, 9) is 3 so you can measure out multiples of 3, i.e. 3, 6, 9, 12, 15. You can only measure out 1L if the two numbers are "relatively prime", i.e. their prime factors don't overlap, for example 16 (2^4) and 9 (3^2).

This is an example of a linear Diophantine equation. In other word, given that a,b,c are integers, I need to find the solution x,y to this equation (which also must be integers).

ax+by=c

Let a=15, and b=9. So we get 15x+9y=c. The GCD(15,9)=3 So we may factor out a 3, 3(5x+3y)=c

Hence any amount we pour must be an integer multiple of 3. 1 is not a multiple of 3, hence we cannot pour out 1 liter.
