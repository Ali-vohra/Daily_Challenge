Consecutive Sum

Every positive integer is the sum of powers of two, with no power repeating. For example,

13=1+4+8=20+22+23.\begin{aligned} 13 & = 1 + 4 + 8 \\ & = 2^0 + 2^2 + 2^3. \end{aligned}13​=1+4+8=20+22+23.​

Is there a quick way to tell whether an integer is the sum of consecutive powers of two, though? Keep reading to find out, or jump ahead to the challenge if you think you already know.

Ancient Egyptians broke down numbers into powers of two as part of their multiplication system. To find powers of two, they just needed to add repeatedly. The fewer powers of two one of the numbers was made of, the better, since that would mean less work to multiply the numbers.

If both numbers were the sum of many powers of two, though, there would be more work for the scribe to do. Numbers that were the sum of consecutive powers of two meant a lot of work. One example is 30×124,30 \times 124,30×124, since 30=2+4+8+1630 = 2 + 4 + 8 + 1630=2+4+8+16 and 124=4+8+16+32+64.124 = 4 + 8 + 16 + 32 + 64.124=4+8+16+32+64.

Let's look at a sum of consecutive powers of two that starts with 20=1,2^0 = 1,20=1, and call it s:s:s:

s=1+2+4+8+16+32. s = 1 + 2 + 4 + 8 + 16 + 32. s=1+2+4+8+16+32.

Now here's s,s,s, doubled:

2s=2+4+8+16+32+64. 2s = 2 + 4 + 8 + 16 + 32 + 64. 2s=2+4+8+16+32+64.

Subtracting 2s−s2s - s2s−s then leaves us with s,s,s, but in a much simpler form than what we started with:

That leaves us with

s=64−1=63.\begin{aligned} s &= 64 - 1 \\ & = 63. \end{aligned} s​=64−1=63.​

Any time we do this with a sum of consecutive powers of two (starting with one), the middle terms cancel out.

What kind of number will a sum like this always be?

More generally, a number xxx is the sum of consecutive powers of aaa if x=an am−1a−1.x = a^n\:\frac{a^m-1}{a-1}.x=ana−1am−1​.

We can therefore build the following algorithm to test if a number is a sum of consecutive powers of aaa:

  • Divide by aaa as often as possible.

  • Multiply by a−1a - 1a−1.

  • Add 1.

  • Divide by aaa as often as possible.

  • The result is one if and only if the original number was a sum of consecutive powers of aaa.

The binary representation corresponds to representing numbers as the sum of powers of two. A number is the sum of consecutive powers of two iff its binary representation consist of a block of 1s and all other digits zero.

It's easy to see that such a number is the difference of the next highest power of 2 and the place value of the last digit 1.

e.g.: 111000 = 1000000 - 1000

As a result a positive number is the sum of consecutive powers of 2 iff it is the difference of two powers of 2.

This is even true for fractional number, if we allow powers of 2 with negative exponents.

For the proposed answers:

16-11 = 5 32-26 = 6 128-65 = 63 128-127 = 1

Only the last option is the difference of two powers of 2 and so only this is the sum of consecutive powers of 2.

Binary representation.

One need only notice that every number has a unique binary representation that explicitly lists the powers of two. Any number represented with all binary digits equal to one is the sum of consecutive powers of two. These numbers will be of the form 2n−12^n - 12n−1. In this example, n=7

One might also consider numbers containing consecutive ones unbroken by a zero. These will be of the form (2m−1)−(2n−1)(2^m - 1) - (2^n - 1)(2m−1)−(2n−1) = 2n(2m−n−1)2^n (2^{m-n} - 1)2n(2m−n−1) , m > n>0 when the powers of two that are present are also consecutive, but not all powers of two in range are present.
