All the Way to the Top

Artificial neural networks (ANNs) can identify patterns in data, like whether or not an animal is in a photo or cancerous growth is in an MRI image. The basic unit of an ANN is the artificial neuron, inspired by simplified mathematical models of the neurons in animal brains.

Just as biological neurons send electrical impulses to other neurons and change their own state based on the pulses they receive, an artificial neuron can be active (■)(\blacksquare)(■) or inactive (□)(\square)(□) based on the information it receives from its inputs.

The single neuron below has 333 inputs, which can be on oroff. Clicking on an input will change its state. Can you find the input pattern that activates the neuron?

Reset

The inputs with green edges drive the neuron toward its active state (■).(\blacksquare).(■). When the pink input is on, it has the opposite effect; it inhibits the neuron. The neuron's output state is determined by summing the inputs: each green edge contributes +1+1+1 and pink one contributes −1.-1.−1. (Only the inputs that are on are counted; inputs switched off have no effect.)

When the sum of the inputs equals or exceeds a particular value, called its bias, the neuron flips from □\square□ to ■.\blacksquare.■. The higher the bias, the greater the input needed to flip the neuron. The bias of the neuron above is +2.+2.+2. It takes 222 positive inputs to flip the neuron to ■.\blacksquare.■.

A single rule governs whether a neuron is activated by its inputs:

This simple rule can give rise to complex behavior when multiple neurons are connected in a neural network. Typically, neurons in a network transform the inputs across a series of "layers."

In the network below, neurons are organized into three layers.

Reset

The inputs make up the bottom layer, the middle layer has two neurons that can be activated by the inputs, and the top layer (the output) consists of one neuron. The inputs to the top neuron are the two neurons in the middle layer.

When all three inputs are on, only the right neuron in the middle layer is active, which isn't sufficient to activate the output neuron at the top. The biases of all three neurons are fixed at +2.+2.+2. Can you figure out how to activate the output neuron?

The solution is to switch off the negative input. This increases the input to the left neuron in the middle layer. Both are activated, which in turn increases the input to the output neuron.

The input combination (on, off, on) is the only one that activates the output neuron. If only one input is on — for example, (((on, off, off))) or (((off, off, on))) — the input to each neuron in the middle layer doesn't equal or exceed the bias +2,+2,+2, so they remain inactive.

Since there are 222 states for each of the three inputs, there are 2×2×2=82\times 2\times 2=82×2×2=8 possible input combinations, and 777 combinations fail to activate the output neuron.

from itertools import product

BIAS = 2
solutions = []
inputSignals = product([1, 0], repeat=5)

def neuron(inputSignal, wiring):
    return 1 if sum([(a*b) for a, b in zip(inputSignal, wiring)]) - BIAS >= 0 else 0

for layer0 in list(inputSignals):
    layer1 = [neuron(layer0, (-1, -1, 1, 1, 1)),
              neuron(layer0, (1, -1, -1, 1, 1)),
              neuron(layer0, (1, 1, -1, -1, 1)),
              neuron(layer0, (1, 1, 1, 1, -1))]
    layer2 = [neuron(layer1, (1, 1, 1, 1)),
              neuron(layer1, (1, 1, 1, -1)),
              neuron(layer1, (1, 1, -1, 1))]
    layer3 = [neuron(layer2, (1, -1, 1)),
              neuron(layer2, (1, 1, 1))]
    output = neuron(layer3, (1, 1))
    solutions.append((output, layer3, layer2, layer1, layer0))

for solution in solutions:
    if solution[0] == 1:
        print([solution[0]])
        print(*solution[1:], sep="\n")
        print()

neuron = [0]*15

bias = { i:2 for i in range(5,15) }
weight = { (5,0):-1, (5,1):-1, (5,2):1, (5,3):1, (5,4):1,
           (6,0):1, (6,1):-1, (6,2):-1, (6,3):1, (6,4):1,
           (7,0):1, (7,1):1, (7,2):-1, (7,3):-1, (7,4):1,
           (8,0):1, (8,1):1, (8,2):1, (8,3):1, (8,4):-1,
           (9,5):1, (9,6):1, (9,7):1, (9,8):1,
           (10,5):1, (10,6):1, (10,7):1, (10,8):-1,
           (11,5):1, (11,6):1, (11,7):-1, (11,8):1,
           (12,9):1, (12,10):-1, (12,11):1,
           (13,9):1, (13,10):1, (13,11):1,
           (14,12):1, (14,13):1 }

count = 0
for k in range(32):
    kk = k
    for n in range(5):
        (kk, neuron[n]) = divmod(kk, 2)

    for n in range(5,15):
        sum = 0
        for m in range(0,n):
            if (n,m) in weight:
                sum += weight[(n,m)]*neuron[m]
        neuron[n] = 1 if sum >= bias[n] else 0

    count += neuron[14]

    print("{:2}: {}{}{}{}{} -> {}".format(k,neuron[0],neuron[1],neuron[2],
          neuron[3],neuron[4],neuron[14]))

print(count)

The possible combinations can be categorized as :

All off - 1 possibility

1 of them on - 5 possibilities

It is obvious that with 0 or 1 input neuron on, it is impossible to switch on the output neuron since we would need at least 2 input neurons to be on. But at first glance , switching with 2 neurons seems unlikely. But let us try it out.

The combinations are (labeling input neurons from 1 to 5 from left to right) : (1,2) , (1,3), (1,4),(1.5), (2,3),(2,4),(2,5).(3.4),(3,5) and (4,5) - 10 in all . Out of the above, it can be seen that (1,4) and (3,4) seem to work (surprisingly !!)

Now let us try 3 inputs on.

The combinations are :

(1,2,3),(1,2.4),(1,2.5),(1,3,4),(1,3,5),(1,4,5),(2,3,4),(2,3,5),(2,4,5) and (3,4,5) - 10 in all. Surprisingly (or not so surprisingly because of the negative weight with possible connections to parity etc), none of the combinations work

Let us examine combinations of 4 inputs

The combinations are : (5 in all)

1, 2 , 3, 4 (leaving out 5) - does not work

1, 3, 4, 5 (leaving out 2) - works

1 , 2, 4, 5 (leaving out 3) - does not work

1, 2 , 3, 5 (leaving out 4) - does not work

and finally

2,3,4,5 - leaving out 1 (works)

Finally, switching on all 5 inputs does not work.

So, the only possibilities that work are :

(1,4), (3,4) , (1,3,4,5) and (2,3,4,5)

    c = [0]*20

    c[2] = n % 2 ; n = n//2
    c[1] = n % 2 ; n = n//2

    # First Layer

    c[6] = 1 * (+c[0] - c[1] - c[2] + c[3] + c[4] > 1)

So far no attempt at a full top-down logical approach, it seems. It's ugly, and brute force might actually be better here, but here goes:

From the intro, we know the third layer needs to be 101.

For the fourth from top layer: To achieve the 0 in the third layer, we can't have all four neurons on. Knowing that, to get the right 1 we can't have the third neuron on, and we need at least two of the others on. Back to that middle 0, we need the rightmost neuron on and one or both of the first two to be on. That is, the fourth layer needs to be one of:

  • 1101
  • 1001
  • 0101

Finally the input layer. Consider the two cases for the last input neuron.
Case off: We need to get one of the first two neurons from the fourth layer on. With our last input off, turning the first output on requires inputs 00110, and turning the second output on requires inputs 10010. These both work out, so 2 solutions so far.
Case on: We need the fourth output neuron on, so we need at least three of the other inputs on. We need the third output neuron off, so those three need to include both the third and fourth inputs. (We have xx111. At this point, there are only four things to try so brute force is fine to me now, but...) If we want to turn on the second output neuron, we need 10111 (and this works). Otherwise, we need to turn on the first output neuron, and for this we just can't set both of the first two input neurons on, so 00111, 10111 (again), and 01111 all need to be checked. 00111 fails, so we have 2 more solutions in this case.

You can use some logic to reduce the number of combination to try. Realize that a neuron will only turn if at a minimum 2 of its connections are on, so any row needs at least 2 neurons on. Then:

  • 0 on: no solutions, trivial
  • 1 on: no solutions, no double counting connections, so cannot turn a neuron on The 2nd row on
  • 3 or 5 on: no solutions: looking at how the green and purple connections are distributed you can only turn at most 1 neuron on in 2nd row. ( of the 5 connections, 3 out of 4 neurons on 2nd row have 3 green and 2 purple, so with 5 on for example those 3 will be off for sure)

So the only combinations that will provide a solution have either 2 or 4 inputs on, reducing number of combinations to try to 15 down from 32).

The possible combinations can be categorized as :

All off - 1 possibility

1 of them on - 5 possibilities

It is obvious that with 0 or 1 input neuron on, it is impossible to switch on the output neuron since we would need at least 2 input neurons to be on. But at first glance , switching with 2 neurons seems unlikely. But let us try it out.

The combinations are (labeling input neurons from 1 to 5 from left to right) : (1,2) , (1,3), (1,4),(1.5), (2,3),(2,4),(2,5).(3.4),(3,5) and (4,5) - 10 in all . Out of the above, it can be seen that (1,4) and (3,4) seem to work (surprisingly !!)

Now let us try 3 inputs on.

The combinations are :

(1,2,3),(1,2.4),(1,2.5),(1,3,4),(1,3,5),(1,4,5),(2,3,4),(2,3,5),(2,4,5) and (3,4,5) - 10 in all. Surprisingly (or not so surprisingly because of the negative weight with possible connections to parity etc), none of the combinations work

Let us examine combinations of 4 inputs

The combinations are : (5 in all)

1, 2 , 3, 4 (leaving out 5) - does not work

1, 3, 4, 5 (leaving out 2) - works

1 , 2, 4, 5 (leaving out 3) - does not work

1, 2 , 3, 5 (leaving out 4) - does not work

and finally

2,3,4,5 - leaving out 1 (works)

Finally, switching on all 5 inputs does not work.

So, the only possibilities that work are :

(1,4), (3,4) , (1,3,4,5) and (2,3,4,5)

The pattern seems to be all even number of inputs containing either 1 and 4 only or 3 and 4 and 5 (with either 1 or 2 in the sense of exclusive OR) being the remaining input that is switched on)
