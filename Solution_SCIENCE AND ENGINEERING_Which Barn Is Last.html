Which Barn Is Last?

Sometimes, computer scientists face problems with few, if any, solutions. Other times, problems seem to have too many solutions. For a computer scientist, it can be surpassingly tricky to design algorithms for problems that have a huge number of possible solutions!

When an algorithm has to navigate the real world, it's common to encounter explosions in the number of possible solutions. Self-driving cars obviously have this problem, and so does your "friendly" autonomous surveillence-and-same-day-delivery drone, the Eye of Amazoo.

In a more open map, there are suddenly three different paths to the orange barn #2.\#2.#2. (You can modify this program by dragging tiles from the top row onto the program in the second row.)

I have tried to provide a logical approach to this problem. Hope you like it!

Note: In the solution, U = Up, D = Down, R = Right and L = Left. Moving diagonally is not allowed usually.

First, to solve this problem we have to find the compulsory no. of moves (same as minimum no. of moves )and the direction of the moves required to go at the barns available from our current position.

Here, we see that in the image below:

We see that the red and the green barn already have 4 minimum moves which means they can not have any more extra moves as the condition states only the possible routes should include 4 moves or fewer. So, as seen in the image above, the red barn should only have the possible routes which have a combination of 1R and 3U moves and for the green barn, the possible routes are which have a combination of 3R and 1U moves.

However, the blue barn can have two additional moves as it has only 2 minimum moves. Also, it cannot have an odd no. of moves in total or an additional 1 move because anyways it would have its minimum no. of moves which are 2U and then for the third move it will either have U or R but if it moves U or R it will not enter the barn or enter before doing the third move and thus it needs to have 4 moves or 2 moves. So if it has 4 moves so the combination of the remaining two moves should have opposite directions so that they cancel each other, and we reach the barn. So we see can have a lot of combinations actually, obviously more than 5 routes.

So we have to see among the red and the green barn now. The image below shows the number of possible routes for the green and red barn.

So as red barn has the least possible routes, the robot now goes to the red barn.

Now from its current position (at the red barn) we see that the green and the blue barn need only 2 compulsory moves to reach, for blue barn it is L and D and for green barn, it is R and D.

So they can have an additional 2 moves or 4 moves in total but not 1 move or in total 3 moves as reasoned above. They have the same no. of possible paths to reach from the red barn due to symmetry (one barn is the same amount far away as the other barn but in opposite directions). But the interesting thing to note that there is a fourth additional column to the right which provides an additional possible path for the green barn.

Thus, the robot reaches the blue barn second and the green barn in the last.
