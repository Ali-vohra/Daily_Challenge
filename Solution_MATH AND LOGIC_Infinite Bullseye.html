Infinite Bullseye

If we were able to keep dividing and coloring the square by this pattern forever, how much of the square would eventually get colored? Keep reading to find out.

Let's think about the steps involved in coloring and dividing the square. Starting with the entire square,

  1. one of the smallest uncolored parts of the square gets divided into fourths (at the beginning, the entire square is uncolored, so the smallest uncolored part is the square itself), and then

  2. one of those fourths gets colored.

Every time, only one of the smallest uncolored parts gets divided, with a fourth of it then colored. This means, with every division into fourths,

  • exactly one part gets colored,
  • another part will eventually get split into fourths, and
  • two parts will never be split or colored.

Instead of starting with a square and dividing it, let's think about building the square in upside-down L-shaped "layers." Each layer is made of a colored square and the two squares of the same size that are not colored. As the square is built, every layer gets a smaller layer with sides half as long placed beside it.

Every layer has a colored square and two uncolored squares of the same size, so 13\frac{1}{3}31â€‹ of every layer is colored. Any shape made from these layers then must have 13\frac{1}{3}31â€‹ of it colored as well. If every layer had a smaller layer with sides half as long placed beside it, we would have a complete square.

Since it's made from these layers, 13\frac1331â€‹ of the square is colored.

Can we do something similar with the dartboard below?
