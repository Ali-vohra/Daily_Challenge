Time Flies When You Turn Around

Your twin set off for a long journey through space, flying away and coming back at 80% of the speed of light. Six years later (from your perspective), they came home â€” but they counted the journey as lasting just 3.63.63.6 years!

From your twin's perspective, time was passing more slowly for you, and they are confused about why they arrived home younger. In today's challenge, you'll discover where your twin's accounting went awry.

In relativity theory, two people moving at high speeds relative to each other disagree on how much time passes between events. This disagreement arises because time passes more slowly on a clock carried by someone you see moving.

This is consistent with your twin arriving home younger, but this seems to give us a paradox when we consider your twin's perspective! Both you and your twin observe time passing more slowly for the other person, but you can't both be older than the other when you come back together. How can we solve this puzzle?

We need two key pieces to solve the puzzle. These are

  • observers moving at different velocities disagree about which things happen at the same time;
  • your twin changes velocity when they turn around to come home, changing their perspective on what happens when.

In relativity, your velocity gives you a particular perspective on when and where events happen, known as a frame. When you change your velocity, you must change this perspective.

Imagine you are on a train moving at a steady speed. In this frame, scenery rolls by outside the window, moving backwards relative to you. If your train is extremely fast, near the speed of light, time will pass more slowly for someone outside the train.

However, if your speed is changing, it makes it more complicated to figure out how much time passes for someone standing beside the track. For example, suppose you see light from a clock striking noon in the town you left. If you try to calculate how long ago the clock struck and how far away it was, your answer will keep changing as long as the train's velocity is changing.

Likewise, when your twin sets out on their journey, they are moving at a steady speed, and they have a consistent view of the time passing on Earth. However, at the turnaround point, they fire their rocket and change their velocity. At this point, their perspective changes.
