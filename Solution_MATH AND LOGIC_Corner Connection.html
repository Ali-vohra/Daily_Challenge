Corner Connection

How can we find A,A,A, the area of the colored triangle, when we're only told two lengths in the figure?

Keep reading to see one way we can do this, or skip straight to today's challenge if you feel ready for a tougher problem.

When first starting a problem, the wishful thinking strategy can help to point you in the right direction. When using it on this problem, you might think as follows:

I know the height of the colored triangle. If I only knew the length of the base, then I could find the area with the formula A=12bh.A = \frac12 bh.A=21​bh.

The base of the colored triangle has a pair of tick marks, and so does the hypotenuse of the middle triangle. This means the two segments have the same length or are congruent. So, now you might think as follows:

I know the length of one leg of the middle triangle. If I only knew the length of the other leg, then I could use the Pythagorean theorem to find the length of the hypotenuse. Then I would have the missing leg of the colored triangle.

Let's see if there is anything in the diagram we haven't thought about yet. One leg of the left triangle has a single tick mark, so it's congruent to the known leg of the middle triangle. We now know both leg lengths of the left triangle; maybe we can somehow use that to help to find the missing leg of the middle triangle.

Two angles of the left and middle triangles are formed by a pair of crossing lines. This means those angles are congruent. They also each have a right angle, so the triangles have two pairs of congruent angles. Then, since all three angle measures add up to 180º180º180º for either triangle, the other pair of angles must also be congruent.

A pair of triangles with three pairs of congruent angles are similar. We know the ratio of the short leg lengths is 43,\frac43,34​, so we could multiply that ratio by 444 to get the missing leg of the middle triangle: 4×43=163.4 \times \frac43 = \frac{16}{3}.4×34​=316​.

We found that leg of the middle triangle, so we could use the Pythagorean theorem to find the hypotenuse:

a2+b2=c242+(163)2=c2 ⋮ \begin{aligned} a^2 + b^2 & = c^2 \\ 4^2 + \left(\frac{16}{3}\right)^2 & = c^2 \\ &\ \vdots \end{aligned} a2+b242+(316​)2​=c2=c2 ⋮​

Sometimes things get messy, and it might help to change course. The left triangle legs don't have fractional lengths. Why don't we use the Pythagorean theorem to find its hypotenuse instead, and then scale that length up to the middle triangle's hypotenuse? The length xxx of the left triangle's hypotenuse is as follows:

32+42=x29+16=x225=x25=x. \begin{aligned} 3^2 + 4^2 & = x^2 \\ 9 + 16 & = x^2 \\ 25 & = x^2 \\ 5 & = x. \end{aligned} 32+429+16255​=x2=x2=x2=x.​

Scaling this length up, the middle triangle's hypotenuse has length 5×43=203.5 \times \frac43 = \frac{20}{3}.5×34​=320​. The colored triangle's missing leg has the same length, so we have all the information we need to answer the question.

The area of the colored triangle is

A=12bh=12×203×4=2×203=403, \begin{aligned} A & = \frac12 bh \\ & = \frac12 \times \frac{20}{3} \times 4 \\ & = 2 \times \frac{20}{3} \\ & = \frac{40}{3}, \\ \end{aligned} A​=21​bh=21​×320​×4=2×320​=340​,​

Consider the big right angled triangle formed by the right vertices of the LHS square with the left bottom vertex of the RHS square. This triangle contains another small right triangle which is contained completely in the LHS square. You will notice that these two triangles are similar.

Corresponding sides of similar triangles bear a constant ratio. The bases of the above pair of triangles are 4 and 10 respectively for the small and the big triangle. The heights are x (unknown) and 6 respectively.

We know from similarity that 4:10=x:6 therefore x=2.4

Now consider the LHS square. Split it into three parts- the upper triangle, the lower triangle (which is the same as the small triangle considered previously), and the shaded part. We know the area of the lower triangle is 0.542.4, which is 4.8

The upper triangle is slightly larger than the lower triangle. If it were equal, i.e. its area were 4.8, the areas of the two triangles combined would be 9.6, subtracting which from the area of the LHS square which is 16, we would get the area of the shaded portion as 16-9.6=6.4

But we know that the upper triangle actually has an area greater than 4.8, hence the area of the shaded region should be less than 6.4, hence 6 is the answer.

Admittedly this approach involves a bit of guesswork, and I would love a more rigorous one.

Reading other people's explanation makes me wonder about the correctness of mine, which is different (although with the same conclusion).

The easiest way to calculate an area of a square is if it has a 90 degree angle, because a triangle with a 90 degree angle is half a rectangle. So the area equals length times height (taking the two sides next to the 90 degree angle) divided by 2.

As there are squares here, we can use only triangles with an 90 degree angle. So start with the yellow area and expand just a little to the top and bottom. That triangle is 4 by 6. Then deduct the two smaller triangles, which also have a 90 degree angle (which MUST be the case because...well...squares!) The area of the slightly too big triangle is is 4 * 6 / 2 = 12.

The top triangle (outside both squares) has the same angles. So the right side has a length of 6 - 4 = 2. And as all sides have that ratio, the bottom side must be 2/6 * 4. A hypotenuse is the longest side in a right triangle. It is opposite the right angle.

Congruent figures (such as angles, polygons, and line segments) are identical in shape and size.

The legs of a right triangle are the two shorter sides.

The Pythagorean theorem states that the squares on the legs of a right triangle add up in area to equal the square on the hypotenuse. Stated algebraically, this is a2+b2=c2a^2 + b^2 = c^2a2+b2=c2, where aaa and bbb are the lengths of the legs and ccc is the length of the hypotenuse.

View wiki page

Figures are similar if they are the same shape. This means the lengths of their corresponding sides are proportional and their angles are equal.
