How Can You Get a Pair?

If we draw two cards from a standard 525252-card deck, how many different pairs can we draw? If we remove a single card from the deck, how many pairs can we draw then? (To be a pair, both cards must be of the same rank. The order of the cards in a pair does not matter.)

Keep reading to see how we can reason about these questions, or jump ahead to today's challenge for a more advanced problem.

Instead of trying to count all possible pairs, let's simplify the problem by choosing one of the 131313 ranks, and count how many pairs we can get from those four cards. We'll count how many pairs of queens we can draw.

Let's say we draw the queen of hearts first. There are three other queens we could draw for the second card to give us a pair, so there are three pairs where the queen of hearts was the first card drawn.

There are also three pairs where the first card is the queen of spades. We already counted one of these pairs earlier, though. The pair queen of hearts, queen of spades is the same as the pair queen of spades, queen of hearts.

In all, we have 4×3=124 \times 3 = 124×3=12 ways we can draw a pair of queens if we're taking into account the order of the cards. We don't care about the order, and dividing by two will get rid of the duplicate pairs. There are 12÷2=612 \div 2 = 612÷2=6 possible pairs of queens.

The same reasoning works for any other rank of cards. There are 131313 different ranks, so we have 13×6=78 13 \times 6 = 78 13×6=78 different pairs.

What if we remove a card from the deck, leaving only 515151 cards? Does that leave us with one fewer possible pair? Two fewer? Let's assume the queen of hearts was removed. This doesn't affect the number of pairs for any other rank, so we can again focus on counting pairs of queens.

There are three queens we can draw first. For each queen drawn first, there are two queens we can draw to complete a pair. That gives us 3×2=63 \times 2 = 63×2=6 pairs where order matters, and dividing by two will eliminate duplicates. That leaves us with 6÷2=36 \div 2 = 36÷2=3 possible pairs of queens.

We had 666 possible pairs of queens before removing the queen of hearts, which left us with 333 fewer possible pairs. So, there are now 78−3=7578 - 3 = 7578−3=75 ways to draw a pair from the deck.

  • (A) Second is also queen (Pair). 2 Queens left.
  • (B) Second is a fives (not queen). 3 Queens and 3 Fives left..

Since all same ranked cards are 4 in number (2 Pairs). It's favorable to get them as it increases the chance to get the same one again. In Scenario 1 just one card is affected. In Scenario 2, 2 cards are affected.

Hence, Scenario 1 is better.
