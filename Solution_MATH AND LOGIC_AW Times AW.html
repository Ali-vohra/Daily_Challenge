# Check every digit for a
for a in range(1, 10):
    # Check every digit except a for w
    for w in set(range(1, 10)) - {a}:
        # Compute aw * aw
        yea = (a * 10 + w) ** 2
        # Extract y (hundreds)
        y = yea // 100
        # Extract e (tens)
        e = yea // 10 % 10
        # Check that yea is indeed three digit long,
        # that y, e, a and w are distinct,
        # and that the last digit of yea is indeed a
        if yea < 1000 and len({y, e, a, w}) == 4 and yea % 10 == a:
            # Print the operation
            print(str(a) + str(w), "+", str(a) + str(w), "=", str(yea))

The output of W^2 defines A, either as a direct product (3^2=9, A=9) or as the last digit of a two-digit product (9^2=81, A=1).

The value of AW is constrained by the fact that AW^2 must be three digits, or less than 1000, so AW must be 31 or less.

You can quickly test the possibilities of W from 1 to 9. Some of them quickly produce invalid results, such as W=1, which results in A=1. That violates the condition that each letter represents a different number. Others result in AW > 31. For instance, if W=2, then A=4 and AW=42 > 31.

This is actually one of the easier problems of this type that has been posted on Brilliant.
