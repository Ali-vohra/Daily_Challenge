Hot Hydrogen Production

Chemical reactions constantly transform reactants to products all around us. We've previously explored how plants use photosynthesis to turn carbon dioxide and water into sugar and oxygen:

6 COX2+6 HX2O→CX6HX12OX6+6 OX2\ce{6CO2 + 6H2O -> C6H12O6 + 6O2}6COX2​+6HX2​O​CX6​HX12​OX6​+6OX2​

and how your body burns sugar for energy, releasing carbon dioxide and water vapor in the process:

CX6HX12OX6+6 OX2→6 COX2+6 HX2O.\ce{C6H12O6 + 6O2 -> 6CO2 + 6H2O}.CX6​HX12​OX6​+6OX2​​6COX2​+6HX2​O.

Seeing these reactions back to back, you might notice something funny about them: they're the same as each other, just in reverse!

While we often write chemical equations as going in one direction like this:

A→B,\ce{A -> B},A​B,

the reality is that almost all reactions can go in both directions. We can express this using a double arrow (⇌)(\ce{<=>})(​), indicating that the reaction can proceed both ways:

A⇌B.\ce{A <=> B}.A​B.

For example, in biological cells, tubulin monomers combine into dimers (the building blocks of the cell's skeleton), but dimers can also break down into monomers: 2 Monomer⇌1 Dimer.\ce{2 Monomer <=> 1 Dimer}.2Monomer​1Dimer.

If reactions can proceed in both directions, how does anything ever get done, chemically speaking? Even if a reaction can proceed in both directions, the reaction has an equilibrium point — a ratio of concentrations of reactants and products that the system will move towards.

If a cell has more monomers than its equilibrium calls for, then the reaction will proceed in the forward direction (i.e. produce more dimers). If it has more dimers than the equilibrium calls for, the reaction will proceed in the reverse direction (i.e. break up dimers).

Fortunately for life and industry, this chemical equilibrium isn't a set value for each reaction — it changes based on the conditions where the reaction is taking place. We can look at an industrial example to see just how this works.

Steam reformation is a process used to produce hydrogen gas from methane (CHX4).(\ce{CH4}).(CHX4​). This is achieved by mixing methane with high-temperature steam, which leads to the following reversible reaction:

To help push the reaction forward, industrialists make use of Le Chatelier's principle — the idea that if the conditions of a system in equilibrium change, the system will respond to (partially) counteract those changes. In this example, the goal of producing additional HX2\ce{H2}HX2​ could be facilitated by removing generated CO:\ce{CO}:CO: the system responds to the removal of CO\ce{CO}CO by producing more, and in the process the reaction is driven forward resulting in more HX2.\ce{H2}.HX2​.

Changing the concentration of chemical species isn't the only way to utilize Le Chatelier's principle — it works for any property of the system. For example, steam reformers could also reduce the pressure in their reactor vessel to promote the forward reaction.

While there are just two reactant gas molecules (((a single CHX4\ce{CH4}CHX4​ and a single HX2O),\ce{H2O}),HX2​O), there are four product gas molecules (((a single CO\ce{CO}CO and three HX2),\ce{H2}),HX2​), so the system responds to a drop in pressure by proceeding towards products, which leads to more gas molecules and counteracts the pressure drop.

This reaction is likely endothermic because, if not, it would have be cheaper to do it with steam not superheated (we are talking about an industrial process where costs count, not a laboratory on). Superheating steam suggests the molecules needs the extra kinetic energy to engage in the reaction.

The emphasis on the reversibility is also a factor: an exothermic reaction produces heat easily dispersed, which is needed to revert the reaction... an exothermic reaction tends to be not sustainable unless its conditions are forced. All chemical reactions are naturally reversible, even the most exothermic ones, because molecules in collision have speed ranges where some will have the values needed to react,... even if the reaction products needs energy to do the inverse reaction, some will have it... but likely not a great percentage. Reversibility of a reaction is then highlighted only when the reverse reaction is important, which is more likely to happen when it does not need external energy to work out, when it is exothermic. But that means the direct reaction is endothermic.

However, these are only circumstantial proofs. There may be other reasons to heat reactants, for instance, to accelerate a reaction (more thermic agitation, more collisions leading to reactions). The problem does not offer reasons for sure to say really which option is the right one.
