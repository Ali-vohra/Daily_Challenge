Clubbing with Trolls

Let's say these two statements are undeniably proven and true:

  • All trolls live under bridges.
  • All things that carry clubs live under bridges.

Does it make sense to conclude this?

Therefore, all trolls carry clubs.

It can be tricky to figure out whether a conclusion like this makes logical sense. To see one helpful way to keep track of the logic involved, keep reading. For a tougher riddle, skip ahead to the challenge.

A visual approach can often help us get a better feel for what's going on. There are three conditions involved:

  • Is a troll.
  • Carries a club.
  • Lives under a bridge.

By representing the three conditions with a Venn diagram, we can make it easier to keep track of how they relate to one another:

All trolls live under bridges, so there aren't any trolls that do not live under bridges. We can show this in our diagram by marking a couple of X's:

All things that carry clubs live under bridges, so let's mark that there are no club-carrying things that do not live under bridges:

The "troll" circle is split into four parts, representing

  1. trolls who do not live under a bridge or carry a club,
  2. trolls who carry a club but do not live under a bridge,
  3. trolls who live under a bridge but do not carry a club, and
  4. trolls who live under a bridge and carry a club.

Our logic lets us rule out two of those parts.

What does that mean for the conclusion? Can we conclude for a fact that all trolls carry clubs?

Can you use a similar strategy with the challenge below?

Based off of these two facts, which statement or statements must be true?

In order to come to the best solution I would propose working from the most inclusive features, since the goal is to determine something that MUST be TRUE, to the most exclusive features operating under the pretense that it will be easier to work by excluding things than including things.

From the premise statements, only one is entirely inclusive: "All trolls live under bridges." while the other can't be entirely inclusive based on the word "Some." There is no need to look any further for the moment.

Now when looking at the answers, identify the answers that claim to be entirely inclusive: 1: All trolls carry clubs. 3: All trolls that carry clubs live under bridges. In trying to match the statements I find that the inclusivity of 1 cannot be matched by the inclusivity of our first premise statement: the nature of living under bridges is irrelevant to a statement solely about carrying clubs. In regards to the second premise statement, there is a disconnect in inclusivity found when substituting our subject "Trolls" in the place of "things": "Some Trolls that carry clubs live under bridges." There is no definite way to like the "All" from 1 to the "Some" from our premise and thus the slight uncertainty disqualifies this statement.

When examining 3: "All trolls that carry clubs live under bridges," we have a match in inclusivity to the first premise statement "All trolls live under bridges." The "Thing" living under a bridge is only being referred to as a "Troll" and no other "Thing" is being referred to. In fact if you remove the phrase "that carry clubs" you are left with the phrase "All trolls live under bridges."
As for the second premise statement "Some things that carry clubs live under bridges.", there is also a match in inclusivity. A "Troll" can be included in the group more generally referred to as "Some things" and due to the fact that our first premise statement has limited the location to only under bridges, there is nothing in the statement to suggest that carrying clubs disqualifies the Trolls from being included. This statement MUST be true.

Disqualifying the next statements is fairly simple. Because 2: "Some trolls don't carry clubs." introduces uncertainty, and neither of our premise statements address the certainty of "ALL Trolls" carrying clubs, only "SOME Things" carrying clubs, there is no way to link their inclusivity and thus it cannot fall under what MUST be true. With 4, using the generality of the current application of the word, you can substitute the word "Every-" for the word "All", and with a slight change for grammatical sake "Everything" becomes "All things". The statement 4: "All things that live under a bridge carries a club." directly contradicts the premise statement "Some things that carry clubs live under bridges.". Thus this cannot fall under what MUST be true.

We know that all trolls must live under bridges, and some things that carry clubs, but aren't necessarily trolls, also live under bridges.

The statement "all trolls that carry clubs live under bridges" must be true in all cases, since all trolls live under bridges, and some trolls may also carry clubs, and they too must live under bridges since they are trolls.

The statement "all trolls carry clubs" could be true but can easily be disproven. For example, we know that all trolls live under bridges, but this doesn't mean that all things living under bridges are trolls, so you could have another creature with a club living under a bridge next to a troll that doesn't have a club, and our 2 facts are satisfied. Alternatively you could have 3 trolls living under a bridge, 2 with clubs and 1 without, and the facts are still satisfied.

Similarly, the statement "some trolls don't carry clubs" could be true, but could also be disproven if all the trolls living under the bridge have clubs. Our information is limited, so this scenario could still be an option as it still satisfies the 2 facts.

Finally, the statement "everything that lives under a bridge carries a club" does not have to be true in all cases. It could be true in the specific case that all the trolls are carrying clubs, but as discussed earlier the 2 known facts are still satisfied if trolls without clubs live under a bridge alongside trolls, and other creatures, with clubs. In this scenario, not everything that lives under a bridge carries a club.
