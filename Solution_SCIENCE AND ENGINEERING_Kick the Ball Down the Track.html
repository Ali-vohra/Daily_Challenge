Kick the Ball Down the Track

Drop a ball onto a flat surface, and it will bounce back up again. Maybe it will return to the height you dropped it from, but even the bounciest of balls will never go higher than that.

However, if you drop a stack of balls, with the lighter ones at the top, the top ball can soar far above its original height. Can you work out how high it goes?

A single bouncing ball cannot exceed the height it was dropped from because it doesn't gain any energy from its collision with the ground. On a perfect bounce with no energy lost or gained, its speed vvv downward before the collision with the ground is equal to its speed vvv upward right after the collision.

But when you drop a stack of balls, the top ball of the stack collides with the ball below it, which in turn collides with the ball directly below it, and so on. In this chain of collisions, one ball can steal energy from the others to go faster. This is how the top ball can go so much higher.

Let's start simple with a golf ball stacked on a bowling ball. We'll work out how much energy the golf ball can gain when the stack is dropped. Instead of dealing in heights, we'll just look at the speeds before and after the collisions.

At the moment the bowling ball collides with the ground, both balls are moving downward at speed v.v.v. Assuming the bowling ball keeps all of its kinetic energy, it will bounce back upwards with the same speed v.v.v. But it immediately collides with the golf ball, which is still moving downwards at speed v.v.v.

Now, the bowling ball is quite a bit more massive than the golf ball. To keep the analysis simple, let's assume that the golf ball is so small that colliding with the bowling ball has no effect on the bowling ball's motion, just as a golf ball bouncing on the hard ground has no effect on the ground.

But there's one crucial difference between the ground and the bowling ball: the ground is stationary, but the bowling ball is headed upward with speed v.v.v. Surely this difference matters. A ball hit by a bat goes a lot farther than a ball that collides with a wall. How do we account for this?

For a brief moment, consider a bowling ball on a collision course with a golf ball, both with speed v.v.v. This is almost the same situation as right after the bowling ball bounces off the ground, except now there's a gap between the bowling ball and the golf ball so we can better see what's going on.

Using the slider above, you can change your perspective of this collision. In particular, you can find a perspective where you are running along with the bowling ball and the bowling ball doesn't appear to move at all. From the point of view, the golf ball is moving towards it at speed 2v.2v.2v.

Now, the golf ball's perfect collision with the bowling ball is just like colliding with the ground. The golf ball keeps all its energy but reverses its velocity, so in the end it bounces back up at speed 2v2v2v relative to the bowling ball.

But we're not interested in the bowling ball's perspective; we want the final speed in our original, "outside" perspective, so we need to flip back again. We see that the bowling ball is still moving upwards at speed v,v,v, so we observe the golf ball's speed as 3v,3v,3v, three times faster than its speed going into the collision.

You can repeat this reasoning for stacks of two, three, or more balls. Just consider each collision in turn, starting with the bottom ball bouncing, and you'll find that the lightest ball can gain an impressive speed from the other balls in the stack.

The idea of shifting perspectives, or "references frames," is a powerful approach in many collision problems, and it also works if you move away from perfectly “elastic” collisions. In more realistic collisions, objects don't always bounce back with the same speed they collided with.

For example, if the bowling ball and the golf ball only retain 70%70\%70% of their relative speed through each collision, you find that the bowling ball is moving up at 0.7v0.7v0.7v and the golf ball hits it with a relative speed of 1.7v.1.7v.1.7v. This loss of energy in each collision is the main reason you can’t use a sufficiently large stack of balls as a low-cost anti-aircraft weapon.

For this problem, we should consider the reference frame of the train. In the reference frame of the train, the ball is moving towards it at 50 m/s\SI{50}{\meter/\second}50 m/s.

Since the train is much heavier than the ball, we can essentially treat the train as the floor and the whole scenario is no different from a ball bouncing on a floor.

Thus, after colliding with the train, the ball should fly away from the train with a relative speed of 50 m/s\SI{50}{\meter/\second}50 m/s. However, since the train is already moving at 50 m/s\SI{50}{\meter/\second}50 m/s, the total speed with respect to an observer standing on the ground is 50+50=100 m/s50+50=\boxed{\SI{100}{\meter/\second}}50+50=100 m/s​

When doing these sorts of problems of collisions, usually easiest to take the frame of reference of the larger mass, if it is much larger than the other (as in this case)

This means I imagined I was on the train (so for me the train is not moving) & the ball is approaching me/train at 50 m/s

A perfectly elastic collision is one where it is assumed no energy is lost ('Newton's cradle' balls get quite close to this) and the train has so much more mass than the ball so can be considered not to have it's speed affected (your car doesn't slow down appreciably when it 'collides' with insects on the motorway) therefore the ball will 'bounce' off the train at the same speed 50m/s

However this 50 m/s is relative to the frame of reference of the train/ me, so the 50m/s of the ball is the 'relative speed' of the ball with respect to the train.

An observer on the ground will see the train carry on moving at 50m/s and the ball bouncing off it at 100 m/s in the same direction (to give a relative speed of 50m/s)

I don't think my explanation would be clear enough for new students of this topic but I'm sure most of you know about relative speeds.

When a mass M approaches another mass m at rest with a velocity u, and M>>m, then after the collision the smaller mass m which was at rest earlier starts moving with velocity 2u and larger mass remains in uniform motion with velocity u. Here u=50m/s therefore 2u=100m/s as the train's mass is very large compared to that of ball. This special case is valid only for inertial frame of references when observer is standing on ground. I was going to post that as the velocity of the train would be a slight (maybe unmeasurable, decrease) then the speed of the ball rebounding at 50 would lead to a slightly higher than 100 relative speed. Then I thought to check my intuition! and I was wrong.

Simply we have two equations to consider for train M, U & final speed V for ball m, u & v with u = 0

Conservation of momentum (mass x velocity) gives total momentum before = total momentum after MU = MV = mv or M(U-V) = mv .........(1)

If we had a perfectly elastic condition then no kinetic energy is lost to heat/sound energy etc.

ie total kinetic energy before = total kinetic energy after (since no mention of the train track being on a slope so we assume potential energy stays the same) 1/2 MU^2 = 1/2 MV^2 + 1/2 mv^2

multiplying by 2 and rearranging -

M (U^2 - V^2) = mv^2

M (U-V) (U+V) = mv^2

Substituting in for M (U-V) from (1) gives mv (U+V) = mv^2

dividing both sides by mv, gives

(U+V) = v

so this shows that v cannot be more than U+V and the maximum value that V can be is = U, (This bit is common sense since if a moving object strikes a stationary object, the moving object can't speed up (that would make for a more interesting world) which gives us 100m/s.

This is an explanation by a twelve year old: In Physics, whatever frame of reference you take you will get the same answer. In this problem, we can choose to take the frame of reference of the bystander. This makes it complicated (not saying it cannot be solved in this way though) but other frames of references makes it easier to solve. Take the frame of reference of the passenger in the front of the train. To the passenger, it looks like the ball is coming toward the train at a speed of 50 mph. When it hits the train and bounces back, it speed of 50 mph doubles. This is because the speed of the train is already 50 mph, and to a person traveling at 50mph ( the train seems stationary to the person) the ball is moving 50 mph when the train is not moving. Really, the train is moving at 50 mph, so we need to add 50 mph to the speed of the ball. This gives us a speed of 100 mph. Hopefully, you understand this. Thanks.
