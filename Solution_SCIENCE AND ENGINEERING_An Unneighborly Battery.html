An Unneighborly Battery

We are surrounded by all sorts of electric devices: some are simple like a light bulb, and some are very complicated like a power grid or a computer. Inside these devices, electricity flows around circuits, carrying energy precisely where it's needed.

Electric current is the coordinated motion of an unimaginable number of electrons, but the rules for how much current passes through a device are surprisingly straightforward.

Electric currents â€” flows of electric charge â€” are driven by differences in voltage. Current flows from positions of high voltage to positions of low voltage â€” similar to how water flows from high elevations to low elevations. In fact, voltage difference along a path in the circuit is the amount of potential energy per charge lost or gained.

Power sources like batteries are sources of voltage; they add potential energy to the moving charges. Resistors, like light bulbs for example, cause the voltage to drop quickly. To keep a steady current, the charges' potential energy splits up according to the circuit's resistance. Along wires, where resistance is low, the voltage barely varies at all. Each item in the circuit produces a change in the voltage, which we usually write Î”V\Delta VÎ”V.

Whether you're looking at a simple circuit or a complicated nest of wires and bulbs with multiple batteries, there are two rules we can follow to piece together these Î”V\Delta VÎ”V's and figure out how much current is supplied.

Voltage rule: The voltage must be consistent at any position, whichever way we calculate it. For example, if there is a circuit with two branches that come back together, the voltage across each branch must be the same.

Current rule: In a steady state, charge cannot build up anywhere in the circuit. This means that if we look at any point in the circuit, the current flowing out must be the same as the current flowing in.

Armed with these rules, we just need some components to slot into our circuits. These components are the building blocks we can use to build any circuit, so engineers represent them with standard symbols. Two of the most important are batteries and resistors:

  • A battery (left) uses chemical energy to add potential energy to the charges flowing through it, maintaining a fixed Î”V\Delta VÎ”V between its ends (regardless of the current flowing through it).
  • A resistor (right) obeys Ohm's law, which says the voltage drop Î”V\Delta VÎ”V across a resistor is proportional to the current III flowing through it: Î”V=âˆ’IR\Delta V = - I RÎ”V=âˆ’IR. The constant RRR is called the resistance of the resistor.

How can we apply these rules? According to the current law, the current is the same all the way around this loop. We can add up the Î”V\Delta VÎ”V's, starting at some arbitrary point P. Ah I remember this, from many decades ago, first semester circuits. You have several choices for this sort of linear system. You can either

  1. use KVL (Kirchoff's voltage law) to make linear equations based on the principle that the sum of all voltages going around every loop in the system is 0 (which is only true assuming there's no changing magnetic flux through that loop of course), which also requires it to be a planar circuit (which this is). You'll of course be solving a n x n linear system with as many variables as the number of loops, which is 2.

  2. use KCL (Kirchoff's current law) to make your linear equations based on the principle that the sum of currents into (or out of) any node is 0 (in continuous systems this is known as the continuity equation). Here you'd be solving a n x n linear system with as many variables as there are nodes, which here is 4. Why would you do this, if you can solve just 2 things instead, what are you, a masochist?

  3. just convert these voltage sources in series with resistances into the thevenin equivalent current sources in parallel with resistances. No linear systems, no matrices to invert or elementary row operations or any such crap. Just bam you're done, in your head. In this case.

It really falls into place immediately if you do 3. The 2 current sources resulting from this method become equal in magnitude (but negatives of each other) when the voltage of one divided by its series resistance is equal to the voltage of the other divided by its series resistance. You just end up with equal and opposite current sources, with some irrelevant resistance in parallel with them, and you don't care about the resistance (calculating the resistance is futile, prepare to be assimilated), you just want the 2 current sources to be equal and opposite. So V1/RS = V2/RL. Multiply both sides by RL and there's your formula for V2, RL*V1/RS.
