Light Energy at Work

One of the first rules we learn in physics is that energy can never be created or destroyed, only transformed.

Much of the modern world is driven by transforming electricity into different forms of power. Speakers turn electrical power into sound. Light bulbs turn electrical power into light. Motors and generators turn electrical power into mechanical power and back, respectively. Keeping track of how electrical power is transferred and transformed is essential to making energy-efficient technologies.

A voltage source, like a battery or a generator, transports electrons through a wire in an electrical appliance. The rate that electric charge flows through a wire is called electric current I.I.I.

Electrical power is produced as billions of individual electrons lose kinetic energy in collisions as they shuffle along. The rate that the electrons' energy is transformed — electrical power PPP in a wire — is the product of the voltage VVV across the wire and current I:I:I: P=IV.P = IV.P=IV.

Light bulbs transform electrical power to light intensity, so the brightness of a bulb depends on the current and voltage in the circuit. A bulb's brightness is directly proportional to the power.

The voltage of a 9 V\SI{9}{\volt}9 V battery forces electrons through a circuit, producing an electrical current. The light bulb resists the flow of electrons, decreasing the current that can flow through the circuit. All wires resist the flow of electrons, but not nearly so much as a light bulb.

The bulb’s resistance sets a limit on the current that can flow through the circuit with the battery’s voltage. If we put another identical bulb in series with the first, the resistance to the electrical current is doubled, and the maximum flow that can be sustained by the battery is cut in half.

Useful equations:

P=IVP = I VP=IV -- Power = Current x Potential Difference

V=IRV = I RV=IR -- Potential Difference = Current x Resistance

First, consider the total resistance of each circuit. Negating the internal resistance of the battery, the total resistance of circuit A, RAR_{A}RA​ = 8Rx8R_{x}8Rx​, where RxR_{x}Rx​ is the resistance of a single filament lamp at a constant temperature. Meanwhile, the total resistance of circuit B, RBR_{B}RB​ = RxR_{x}Rx​.

Let us now consider the two equations listed at the start of this answer. If we rearrange the second equation, then I=VRI = \frac{V}{R}I=RV​. If we then substitute this into the first equation, we get P=V2RP = \frac{V^{2}}{R}P=RV2​.

The two circuit arrangements both have a single battery with an emf of 9 volts. From Kirchoff's Second Law, the sum of emfs must equal the sum of the potential differences across a series circuit or loop. The potential difference across the nine filament lamps in circuit A must therefore equal the potential difference across the single lamp in circuit B.

The power dissipated by circuit A (with negligible internal resistance and negligible resistance from the wires):

PA=V2RP_{A} = \frac{V^{2}}{R}PA​=RV2​ = 818Rx\frac{81}{8R_{x}}8Rx​81​

The power dissipated by circuit B:

PB=V2RP_{B} = \frac{V^{2}}{R}PB​=RV2​ = 81Rx\frac{81}{R_{x}}Rx​81​ = 8PA8 P_{A}8PA​

More power is therefore dissipated by circuit B than by circuit A.

less resistance means more current with the same voltage.

Ohm's law V=IR I=V/R R=V/I

Power is proportional to the square of the current or voltage for a given resistance.

P= VI = (I^2)*R = (V^2)/R

If I use an example C (like B, but with 9 bulbs to simplify the arithmetic)

Arrangement A (one bulb) V= 9 volts R = R I = V/R = 9/R

P= V*I =  9*I = 9 * (9/R) = 81/R

Also: P = (I^2)*R = ((9/R)^2) * R = 81/(R^2)) * R = 81/R Also: P = (V^2)/R = 81/R

Arrangement C (9 bulbs) First, considering each bulb individually: V= 1 volt (series connection, voltage dropped across equally across each bulb) R = R I = V/R = 1/R

P = (V^2)/R = 1/R

Also: P= VI = 1 * (1/R) = 1/R Also: P = (I^2)R = ((1/R)^2) * 1 = 1/R

so, for all 9 bulbs: P = 9/R

Similarly, Arrangement C, considering all nine bulbs together: V= 9 volts Resistance = 9R I = V/R = 9/(9R) = 1/R

P = (V^2)/R = (9^2)/(9*R) =81/(9*R) = 9/R

Also: P= VI = 9 * (1/R) = 9/R Also: P = (I^2)R = ((1/R)^2) * 9*R = 9/R

So, in conclusion: Arrangement A (one bulb) P = 81/R Arrangement C (9 bulbs in series) P = 9/R

Arrangement A uses 9 times as much power as arrangement C
