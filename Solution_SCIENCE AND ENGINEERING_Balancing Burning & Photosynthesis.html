Balancing Burning & Photosynthesis

The vast majority of cars need to burn fuel in order to operate, but how does fuel store energy and how does burning release it? Chemical energy is stored in molecular bonds, and burning fuel breaks those bonds, freeing up some of the energy to power your car.

Consider octane (CX8HX18),(\ce{C8H18}),(CX8​HX18​), an important component of the gasoline used in cars. When octane reacts with oxygen, the energy in bonds is released and the atoms reform into new molecules with lower energy bonds — COX2\ce{CO2}COX2​ and HX2O\ce{H2O}HX2​O — while the excess bond energy is released as heat.

"But wait," you might protest, "between octane and OX2\ce{O2}OX2​ there are 222 oxygen atoms, but between carbon dioxide and water there are 3.3.3." If this seems improper to you, you're right, and that's why we can't be satisfied with merely knowing the identity of reactants.

Reactions can't create, destroy, or transform the identity of atoms — we're doing chemistry, not alchemy, after all.

This equation has the right chemicals present, but we need to do some arithmetic to balance it. We need to figure out how many of each chemical species we need so that the total number of each atom is the same on either side of the equation. Since octane is the most constrained molecule (it always has 8 carbon atoms for every 18 hydrogen atoms), we'll try to use one of it and find the number of each of the other molecules we need to balance it: CX8HX18+x OX2→y HX2O+z COX2.\ce{C8H18 + xO2 -> yH2O + zCO2}.CX8​HX18​+xOX2​​yHX2​O+zCOX2​. To find the other coefficients, we can break the equation down element by element. For example, since octane (8(8(8 carbon atoms per molecule))) and carbon dioxide (1(1(1 carbon atom per molecule))) are the only carbon-containing molecules, if the carbon atoms are going to balance them, it has to be the case that z=8.z=8.z=8. Applying similar logic to hydrogen and oxygen, we get 18=2y2x=y+2z.\begin{aligned} 18 &= 2y \\ 2x &= y + 2z. \end{aligned}182x​=2y=y+2z.​ The hydrogen equation shows that y=9,y = 9,y=9, which we can plug into the oxygen equation (((along with z=8)z = 8)z=8) to get x=12.5,x = 12.5,x=12.5, which leads to CX8HX18+12.5 OX2→9 HX2O+8 COX2.\ce{C8H18 + 12.5O2 -> 9H2O + 8CO2}.CX8​HX18​+12.5OX2​​9HX2​O+8COX2​. Not many people like decimals, and even fewer people like half oxygen molecules, so we can multiply the whole thing by 222 to get our final balanced equation for the combustion of octane: 2 CX8HX18+25 OX2→18 HX2O+16 COX2.\ce{2C8H18 + 25O2 -> 18H2O + 16CO2}.2CX8​HX18​+25OX2​​18HX2​O+16COX2​.

The first step of this problem is to balance the chemical equation for photosynthesis. We can balance it around glucose, which is the most constrained molecule: x COX2+y HX2O→CX6HX12OX6+z OX2\ce{xCO2 + yH2O -> C6H12O6 + zO2}xCOX2​+yHX2​O​CX6​HX12​OX6​+zOX2​ Balancing the carbon atoms and hydrogen atoms, we get: x=62y=12\begin{aligned} x &= 6 \\ 2y &= 12 \end{aligned}x2y​=6=12​ 6 COX2+6 HX2O→CX6HX12OX6+z OX2\ce{6CO2 + 6H2O -> C6H12O6 + zO2}6COX2​+6HX2​O​CX6​HX12​OX6​+zOX2​ Balancing the oxygen atoms, we get: 12+6=6+2zz=6\begin{aligned} 12+6 &= 6+2z\\ z &= 6 \end{aligned}12+6z​=6+2z=6​ So the balanced chemical equation for photosynthesis is: 6 COX2+6 HX2O→CX6HX12OX6+6 OX2\ce{6CO2 + 6H2O -> C6H12O6 + 6O2}6COX2​+6HX2​O​CX6​HX12​OX6​+6OX2​ And we see that photosynthesis produces 666 molecules of oxygen for each molecule of glucose produced.

Since a glucose molecule weighs about 666 times as much as an oxygen molecule, but 666 times more oxygen molecules are produced, that means that the masses of the glucose and oxygen produced are roughly equal. So photosynthesis produces about 1 kg\SI{1}{\kilo\gram}1 kg of oxygen per 1 kg\SI{1}{\kilo\gram}1 kg of glucose.
