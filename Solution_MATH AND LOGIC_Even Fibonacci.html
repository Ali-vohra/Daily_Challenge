Even Fibonacci

We can build the Fibonacci sequence by starting with a 000 and a 111 and then applying a rule: each term of the sequence is the sum of the previous two terms.

That makes the third term 0+1=1,0 + 1 = 1,0+1=1, the fourth term 1+1=2,1 + 1 = 2,1+1=2, the fifth 1+2=3,1 + 2 = 3,1+2=3, and so on:

0,1,1,2,3,5,8,13,21,… 0, 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, \ldots 0,1,1,2,3,5,8,13,21,…

The terms of the sequence grow larger and larger, but are there any hidden patterns in it? Keep reading to see one, or jump to the challenge for a related question.

Instead of looking at the terms of the sequence themselves, let's just ask ourselves whether they're even or odd:

0,1,1,2,3,5,8,13,21,…E,O,O,E,O,O,E,O,O,…\def\arraystretch{1.5}\begin{array}{cccccccccc} 0, & 1, & 1, & 2, & 3, & 5, & 8, & 13, & 21, & \ldots \\ \text{E}, & \text{O}, & \text{O}, & \text{E}, & \text{O}, & \text{O}, & \text{E}, & \text{O}, & \text{O}, & \ldots \end{array}0,E,​1,O,​1,O,​2,E,​3,O,​5,O,​8,E,​13,O,​21,O,​……​

There definitely seems to be a pattern here, but will it keep repeating forever? To answer this question, we can think about what "even" and "odd" mean in terms of division. When we divide an even integer by two, we always get a remainder of zero. Dividing an odd integer by two always leaves us with a remainder of one.

When we add two integers, we can find the remainder of the sum (upon division by two) by adding the two integers' remainders and then dividing that sum. For example, the fifth and sixth terms of the Fibonacci sequence are 333 and 5.5.5. They each leave a remainder of one after we divide them by two. To find the remainder of the seventh term, we can add 1+1=21 + 1 = 21+1=2 and find its remainder, which is zero:

0,1,1,2,3,5,8,13,21,…0,1,1,0,1,1,0,1,1,…\def\arraystretch{1.5}\begin{array}{rrrrrrrrrr} 0, & 1, & 1, & 2, & 3, & 5, & 8, & 13, & 21, & \ldots \\ 0, & 1, & 1, & 0, & 1, & 1, & 0, & 1, & 1, & \ldots \end{array}0,0,​1,1,​1,1,​2,0,​3,1,​5,1,​8,0,​13,1,​21,1,​……​

Does this remainder pattern continue forever? Can you predict whether a certain term in the sequence will be even or odd?

    The simplest way is to think in operations module 2, or binary operations. We start with 0 and 1, and take the sequence from there:

    0 1 1 0 1 1 0 1 1 0 1 1 0 1 1 ⋯0\,1\,1\,0\,1\,1\,0\,1\,1\,0\,1\,1\,0\,1\,1\,\cdots011011011011011⋯

    This translates the simple fact that even plus odd gives odd, odd plus odd gives even, odd plus even gives odd, and the sequence repeats again. It is easy to infer from this that even numbers will happen in periods of 3, starting with the position 1, meaning the Nth number is even if it is of the form:

    3n+13n+13n+1

    ...or if the remainder of its division by 3 is 1. So, lets see which numbers are of that form:

    N3×n+r173×5+2183×6+0193×6+1203×6+2\begin{array}{c|c}N&3\times n+r\\ 17&3\times5+2\\ 18&3\times6+0\\ \boxed{19}&3\times6+1\\ 20&3\times6+2\\ \end{array}N171819​20​3×n+r3×5+23×6+03×6+13×6+2​

    Note that this kind of exercise can be made for any module or multiplicative factor. For instance, for 3:

    0 1 1 2 0 2 2 1 0 1 1 2 0 2 2 1 0 1 ⋯0\,1\,1\,2\,0\,2\,2\,1\,0\,1\,1\,2\,0\,2\,2\,1\,0\,1\,\cdots011202210112022101⋯

    That means every 4th Fibonacci number after 0, is a multiple of 3, not to mention other patterns (with period 8)
