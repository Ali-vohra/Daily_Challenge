Parallel Cut

How can we find A,A,A, the area of the colored triangle, when we're only told two lengths in the figure?

Keep reading to see one way we can do this, or skip straight to today's challenge if you feel ready for a tougher problem.

When first starting a problem, the wishful thinking strategy can help to point you in the right direction. When using it on this problem, you might think,

"I know the height of the colored triangle. If I only knew the length of the base, then I could find the area with the formula A=12bh.A = \frac12 bh.A=21​bh."

The base of the colored triangle has a pair of tick marks, and so does the hypotenuse of the middle triangle. This means the two segments have the same length, or are congruent. So now, you might think,

"I know the length of one leg of the middle triangle. If I only knew the length of the other leg, then I could use the Pythagorean theorem to find the length of the hypotenuse. Then I would have the missing leg of the colored triangle."

Let's see if there is anything in the diagram we haven't thought about yet. One leg of the left triangle has a single tick mark, so it's congruent to the known leg of the middle triangle. We now know both leg lengths of the left triangle; maybe we can somehow use that to help to find the missing leg of the middle triangle.

Two angles of the left and middle triangles are formed by a pair of crossing lines. This means those angles are congruent. They also each have a right angle, so the triangles have two pairs of congruent angles. Then, for either triangle, all three angle measures add up to 180 ∘,180^{\, \circ},180∘, so the other pair of angles must also be congruent.

A pair of triangles with three pairs of congruent angles are similar. We know the ratio of the short leg lengths is 43,\frac43,34​, so we could multiply that ratio by 444 to get the missing leg of the middle triangle: 4×43=163.4 \times \frac43 = \frac{16}{3}.4×34​=316​.

We found that leg of the middle triangle, so we could use the Pythagorean theorem to find the hypotenuse:

a2+b2=c242+(163)2=c2  ⋮ \begin{aligned} a^2 + b^2 & = c^2 \\ 4^2 + \left(\frac{16}{3}\right)^2 & = c^2 \\ &\ \ \vdots \end{aligned} a2+b242+(316​)2​=c2=c2  ⋮​

Sometimes things get messy, and it might help to change course. The left triangle's legs don't have fractional lengths. Why don't we use the Pythagorean theorem to find its hypotenuse instead, and then scale that length up to the middle triangle's hypotenuse? We have

32+42=c29+16=c225=c25=c. \begin{aligned} 3^2 + 4^2 & = c^2 \\ 9 + 16 & = c^2 \\ 25 & = c^2 \\ 5 & = c. \end{aligned} 32+429+16255​=c2=c2=c2=c.​

Scaling this length up, the middle triangle's hypotenuse has length 5×43=203.5 \times \frac43 = \frac{20}{3}.5×34​=320​. The colored triangle's missing leg has the same length, so we have all the information we need to answer the question:

The area of the colored triangle is

A=12bh=12×203×4=2×203=403, \begin{aligned} A & = \frac12 bh \\ & = \frac12 \times \frac{20}{3} \times 4 \\ & = 2 \times \frac{20}{3} \\ & = \frac{40}{3}, \\ \end{aligned} A​=21​bh=21​×320​×4=2×320​=340​,​

Let x,y be the side lengths of triangle A.

There are only 2 equations we need to write to solve this problem:

1) The difference between the area of the 'bigger' triangle and the orange triangle is 42:

      42 = (x+3)*(y+4)/2 - x*y/2  => ... => 4x + 3y = 72

2) The 2 triangles have the same angles, so their sides follow the same proportions:

      x / y = (x+3)/(y+4) => ... => y = x * 4/3

Solving the system formed of the 2 above equations, we get that

x = 9 and y = 12

=> A = 9*12/2 = 54

A hypotenuse is the longest side in a right triangle. It is opposite the right angle.

Congruent figures (such as angles, polygons, and line segments) are identical in shape and size.

The legs of a right triangle are the two shorter sides.

The Pythagorean theorem states that the squares on the legs of a right triangle add up in area to equal the square on the hypotenuse. Stated algebraically, this is a2+b2=c2a^2 + b^2 = c^2a2+b2=c2, where aaa and bbb are the lengths of the legs and ccc is the length of the hypotenuse.

Figures are similar if they are the same shape. This means the lengths of their corresponding sides are proportional and their angles are equal.

Two lines are parallel if they are in the same plane but will never intersect.
